How to read APRA's quarterly statistics

27 June 2026 · synthesised from 6 sources

APRA publishes a quarterly statistical suite covering superannuation, life insurance, general insurance and private health. Here's what each set of numbers actually tells a practitioner — and what signals to watch.

APRA publishes a quarterly statistical suite covering all major regulated sectors — life insurance, general insurance, private health, and superannuation. These releases are publicly available but dense — this piece walks through what each data set measures, what the key trends look like, and what signals an actuarial or group life professional should extract.

The APRA quarterly statistical suite

The APRA quarterly statistics are a family of publications released each quarter with a 6–8 week lag. They cover:

  • Life Insurance Performance Statistics — premiums, claims, profit/(loss) by product class (term life, TPD, income protection/DI, funeral).
  • General Insurance Performance Statistics — premiums, claims, combined ratios by class (home, motor, CTP, commercial lines).
  • Private Health Insurance Performance Statistics and Activity Statistics — membership, coverage, benefit payments, hospital vs ancillary splits.
  • Superannuation: Industry, Fund-Level, and Product Statistics — AUM, contributions, benefit payments, investment returns by asset class and fund type (MySuper vs choice, industry vs retail).

Each publication runs to dozens of tables; most practitioners extract 3–5 key indicators. Knowing which indicators matter — and why — is the value-add.

Life insurance: loss ratio and the DI recovery story

For life insurance, the headline metric is the loss ratio (net incurred claims ÷ net earned premium), separately disclosed for each product class. A loss ratio below 100% means the product is profitable on an underwriting basis before expenses; above 100% means the insurer is paying out more in claims than it earns in premium.

The DI (disability income / income protection) saga is the defining story of Australian life insurance in the 2010s. DI loss ratios ran well above 100% for most of the decade — APRA data showed the industry losing billions on IP/DI cumulatively, driven by claim incidence rates and benefit durations that were far higher than pricing assumed. APRA intervened with the Disability Income Insurance Sustainability Measures from 2021, forcing repricing, benefit period caps (five-year maximum on new policies, with some individual exemptions under transitional arrangements), and stronger cancellability provisions.

By 2025–26, the quarterly data will show whether that remediation is taking hold. Watch the DI loss ratio trend: a sustained sub-100% result for multiple consecutive quarters is the signal that repricing has worked. Any rebound toward 110%+ would suggest new claim trends (long COVID sequelae, mental health, economic downturn) are re-stressing the portfolio.

For group life specifically, the life insurance quarterly aggregates include group term and group IP/TPD, though they're not always broken out separately from retail. Group life practitioners typically supplement APRA data with direct cedant/reinsurer claims experience — APRA data gives the industry context but not the granularity needed for pricing a specific group scheme.

General insurance: the combined ratio

The combined ratio is the defining profitability metric in general insurance. It is the sum of:

  • Loss ratio: net claims incurred ÷ net earned premium
  • Expense ratio: management expenses ÷ net earned premium

A combined ratio below 100% indicates underwriting profit; above 100% means the insurer is losing money from underwriting (and must rely on investment income — "float" — to be profitable overall). Catastrophe events (bushfires, cyclones, floods) drive acute combined ratio spikes. The APRA quarterly data shows this clearly: lines like home and commercial property oscillate significantly with cat experience.

For the June 2026 quarter, watch for the flow-through of any Q1 cat activity (the Australian summer storm/flood season runs October–March). Commercial lines profitability — particularly cyber, D&O, and liability — is also worth tracking given ongoing hardening in those markets globally.

Private health insurance: membership and benefits

APRA's private health statistics track both participation (number of insured lives, hospital vs general treatment coverage) and financial performance (premium income, benefit payments, net margin). The post-COVID normalisation trend is key: elective procedure backlogs drove elevated benefits payments in 2022–23 as deferred surgeries were completed; by 2025–26, the question is whether claims have settled back to a pre-COVID trend or established a new (higher) baseline.

Hospital benefits are the dominant cost driver — typically 75%+ of total benefits paid. Ancillary (optical, dental, physio) benefits have historically been more stable but are growing as members use accumulation-based ancillary limits more actively. Claims ratio by fund (benefits paid ÷ premium revenue) is the headline financial metric; a ratio above ~85% is challenging given the administrative loading required.

One structural trend worth monitoring in the APRA data: fund concentration. The industry has seen a long run of consolidation, with smaller funds exiting or merging into larger ones. APRA fund-level data lets you track whether this is continuing and which funds are gaining or losing membership share.

Superannuation: the fund performance metrics

The APRA quarterly super statistics are the basis of APRA's Your Future, Your Super performance test. The key metrics for fund-level assessment are:

  • Net return after fees and tax: the composite return figure used in the performance benchmarking test. Funds that underperform their benchmark by 50bp over 8 years fail the test and must notify members.
  • Asset allocation: the split across equities, fixed income, infrastructure, alternatives, and cash. Asset allocation drives most of the variance in long-run returns.
  • Member-level data: APRA's product statistics now include account-level segmentation — allowing comparison across account types and fee structures.

Each quarterly release reflects equity market performance through the reference quarter. Fixed income asset values rise when rates fall and vice versa — so a quarter of rate hikes will weigh on bond allocations. Funds with higher allocations to unlisted infrastructure and private credit see more muted short-run mark-to-market volatility.

What this means for group life pricing and reserving

For an actuary working in group life, these four data sets interact. Super fund consolidation (from the super statistics) changes the composition of group insurance arrangements — larger, more sophisticated funds run their own experience analyses and push harder on pricing. Life insurance loss ratios (from the life stats) set the context for cedant experience conversations. Interest rates (see the RBA article in this issue) affect both the discount rate used for reserving and the investment income component of premium rates. PHI trends matter for workforces with embedded health benefits.

The quarterly cadence is fast enough to catch emerging claims trends within a year, but the small sample size in any one quarter means short-run noise is high. Extract the trend — rolling four-quarter averages for loss ratios — rather than reacting to any single quarter.
