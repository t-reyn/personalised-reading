The RBA's Tightening Cycle: Three Hikes In, What Comes Next?

27 June 2026 · synthesised from 5 sources

The Reserve Bank has raised the cash rate for a third consecutive meeting. What's driving it, how it flows through to your mortgage and super, and when the market thinks rates might finally fall.

Australia's Reserve Bank has surprised many forecasters by lifting the cash rate for a third meeting in a row. After a brief easing cycle in late 2025, the RBA pivoted back to tightening as inflation proved stickier than expected. Here's what it means and what comes next.

The cash rate: what it is and why it matters

The RBA cash rate is the interest rate the Reserve Bank sets for overnight loans between commercial banks. It's the base rate in the economy — every other interest rate, from your home loan to a term deposit, is priced relative to it. When the RBA raises the cash rate, banks face higher costs to fund themselves and almost always pass that on to borrowers (within days, in the case of variable mortgages), while savers see better returns on deposits (though with a lag).

At the time of the three consecutive hikes, Australia's cash rate is meaningfully above its pandemic-era lows. The mechanism of monetary policy transmission means rate moves take time to fully bite: research suggests the peak effect on household spending arrives 12–18 months after a rate change, as fixed-rate mortgages roll off onto higher variable rates, businesses reprice loans, and consumers adjust budgets.

Why the RBA hiked again

The RBA's mandate is to keep underlying (trimmed-mean) inflation within a 2–3% target band on average over the medium term. Trimmed-mean inflation is the RBA's preferred measure: it strips out the most volatile price movements at both ends of the distribution (think: a sudden spike in fuel prices or a temporary fruit shortage) to reveal the underlying inflation trend. It's less noisy than headline CPI and harder to dismiss as a one-off.

May 2026 data sent a mixed signal: headline CPI showed some softening in goods prices, but services inflation — driven by rent, insurance, and non-tradeable services — remained elevated and stickier than the Board wanted to see. The RBA's June 2026 Bulletin flagged that geopolitical risk adds another complication: trade disruptions and energy price volatility from global flashpoints have kept imported inflation elevated and made the path back to target less predictable. In that environment, the Board chose to err on the side of further tightening rather than risk letting inflation expectations drift.

Geopolitical wildcard

The RBA's June 2026 Financial Stability analysis noted that geopolitical tensions — ongoing conflicts affecting energy supply chains, fragmentation of global trade, and the risk of coordinated sanctions — have meaningfully increased the uncertainty around the inflation outlook. Australia's open economy is exposed: we import fuel, manufactured goods, and capital, all of which carry a geopolitical risk premium right now. This means the RBA cannot rely purely on domestic demand data when calibrating rates; global price shocks can re-ignite inflation even if local spending slows.

When might rates fall?

Markets and analysts currently expect the RBA to hold or make at most one more small hike before pausing. Rate cuts are being priced by futures markets in late 2026 to early 2027 — but this timeline has shifted outward multiple times as inflation has persisted longer than expected. Canstar's analysis of the 2026 rate path notes that two conditions need to be met: underlying inflation must be clearly trending back toward 3%, and the labour market must show some loosening (lower vacancy rates, moderating wage growth) so the RBA is confident demand is cooling.

The asymmetry matters: cutting too early and re-igniting inflation would be costly and embarrassing; holding too long inflicts unnecessary pain on mortgage holders. The RBA is navigating this with forward-guidance statements about being "data-dependent" — meaning each meeting is a fresh assessment rather than a pre-committed path.

How it flows into your financial picture

  • Variable mortgages: each 25bp hike adds roughly $50–60/month to a $500k loan. Three hikes at 25bp each compounds quickly.
  • Superannuation: higher rates compress asset values in the short run (bonds fall as yields rise; growth assets re-price on higher discount rates). But they improve the long-run expected return for new money entering super — a silver lining for accumulation-phase members.
  • Cash and term deposits: savers benefit. At-call savings accounts and term deposits are at their best real yields in over a decade for those not carrying debt.
  • Property: higher borrowing costs reduce purchasing power and dampen price growth. The Q1 2026 APRA data on housing investor lending will show how investor activity is responding — watch this space.

The bottom line

Three consecutive hikes mean the RBA is in an active tightening posture — not a one-off move. The case for cuts depends on inflation returning to target, and that hasn't happened yet. If you're making financial decisions in 2026 (refinancing, investing, setting super strategy), the working assumption should be "higher for longer" until the data clearly shifts. Build buffers, lock in what you can, and watch the trimmed-mean number when the next CPI print lands.
