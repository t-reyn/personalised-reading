The Specific Action That Predicts Whether a New User Comes Back

25 July 2026 · foundations piece

A signup count and a week-one retention number both hide the same missing metric: the one action, taken in a new user's first session, that separates the ones who become regulars from the ones who quietly vanish, and how to find yours with a spreadsheet and the users you already have.

Two people sign up for the same app on the same day. Six months later, one opens it weekly and the other can't recall installing it, and the gap between them was usually set in their first ten minutes, not their first ten weeks. Most solo builders track two numbers religiously: total signups, and what share of a cohort is still around at day 30. Almost none of them can name the one action, taken early, that splits the stayers from the quiet leavers. That action has a name, the activation event, and finding yours is the highest-leverage thing you can do with a spreadsheet and the users you already have.

An account isn't a commitment

Signing up creates a row in a database, not a habit. Everyone who has just created an account is still deciding whether the product is worth a second visit, and lumping them in with users who've been active for months hides the split entirely. Facebook's early growth team is widely credited with finding that new users who added seven friends within their first ten days went on to retain at dramatically higher rates than those who didn't, not more friends eventually, that specific number inside that specific window. Twitter's equivalent was following roughly thirty accounts. Dropbox's was a single file saved into a single folder on a single device. The point isn't to copy any of those exact numbers into your own app; they aren't universal. They're just proof the pattern exists: somewhere in the first days, there is usually one measurable action that a large share of your long-term users take and a large share of your churned users never do.

Your own early users already have the answer

You don't need Facebook's user count to find yours. Pull two lists: users still active two months after signup, and users who signed up in the same window and quietly stopped. Compare what the first group did in their very first session that the second group never did, not total time spent, not feature count, one specific action. If you won those first users the unscalable way, one at a time, by hand, you're sitting on the best dataset for this you'll ever get: message half of them directly and ask what made it click, instead of guessing from a funnel too thin to trust.

Every screen before that moment is a leak

Once you know the action, treat the gap between signup and reaching it as a single number worth minimising: time-to-value. Every screen inserted before a new user reaches that moment is a chance to lose someone who was never actually rejecting the product, they just never got there. A profile photo prompt, a permissions request, a five-question goal-setting quiz, an invite-a-friend nudge: each might matter eventually, but stacked in front of the activation event they're pure friction. The fix isn't deleting them, it's re-ordering them to land after the user has felt the product work at least once. Nothing has to be cut; most of it just doesn't belong first.

A worked example

Say a habit-tracking app finds that users who log one activity within 48 hours of signing up retain at three times the rate, by day 60, of users who don't, regardless of how many screens they've clicked through otherwise. That's the activation event: one logged activity, inside 48 hours. If the current onboarding runs account creation, a permissions prompt and a goal-setting quiz before the user ever reaches the logging screen, that's three chances to lose someone before they've experienced the one thing that predicts they'll stay. Move the quiz and the permissions prompt to after the first log, and the only thing standing between signup and value is the thing that's meant to be there.

The users who already told you the answer

This week, pull the list of early users and split it into two piles: still opening the app, and gone quiet. Look for the one thing session one of the first pile has that the second pile doesn't, not time spent, not features touched, one action. Once it's named, time the current onboarding flow against it in steps, not minutes, and move anything that isn't essential to reaching it onto the other side of that line.
