Every Channel Decays — The Manual Work That Buys You Time First

4 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Every acquisition channel decays from the day you find it. The founders who last are the ones who do the unscalable, manual work first, and use it to build the taste that picks the next channel well.

The channel is never the moat

Every acquisition channel you'll ever touch starts strong and ends weak. Growth investor Andrew Chen calls this the "law of shitty clickthroughs": whatever channel you're using right now, display ads, cold email, App Store search, organic social, is decaying as you read this, and will keep decaying until it's barely worth the effort. Chen identifies three forces driving the decline: novelty wears off as audiences get used to the format, competitors pile into anything that's working, and the channel scales into audiences who were never a great fit to begin with. Banner ads went from a clickthrough rate above 2% in the 1990s to a rounding error today, for exactly this reason.

The implication for anyone building an indie app or side project is uncomfortable: whatever channel gets you your first hundred users won't get you your next thousand at the same cost. Chasing a "growth hack" is chasing a curve that's already bending down. The founders who last aren't the ones who found a secret channel. They're the ones who bought themselves time with something that doesn't scale at all.

What Airbnb and Stripe did instead

Paul Graham's essay "Do Things that Don't Scale" collects this pattern across a dozen well-known startups, and it's worth being specific about what they actually did, because "hustle harder" isn't a tactic. Airbnb's founders went door to door in New York, personally photographing listings and coaching hosts on pricing, work that obviously couldn't scale to a national market, but that got the first hundred listings good enough to convert visitors. Stripe's founders did what's now called the "Collison install": instead of emailing a signup link, they'd sit down next to a prospective user and install the payment integration themselves, on the spot. Facebook launched at Harvard only, deliberately refusing to open sign-ups anywhere else until the network effect inside one campus was strong enough to carry word of mouth to the next one.

None of these are growth channels in the marketing sense. They're a founder trading their own time, one relationship at a time, for retention and referral behaviour no ad platform can manufacture. That's the trade: it buys a runway of real usage and real advocates before you need a channel to scale at all.

Building on what you already know

This connects directly to the manual outreach already covered for getting your first ten users: go to where your buyers already are, reach out individually, watch them use the product. The narrow-launch pattern is the next layer up. Don't just do that outreach broadly; do it inside one specific, bounded community first, one gym, one suburb's Facebook group, one Discord server, and don't expand until that group is genuinely saturated. Facebook didn't launch at "universities". It launched at Harvard, then Stanford, then the rest of the Ivy League, each wave validated before the next opened.

It also explains why product taste compounds the way it does. Founders doing Collison installs and door-to-door listings aren't just collecting users. They're sitting close enough to the friction to develop an instinct for what "good" looks like, before any dashboard or survey could tell them. That taste is what lets them pick the right channel later, and recognise the moment it's started to decay.

What to do this week

Pick one bounded group, not a market segment, an actual group of people who already talk to each other: a specific gym, a specific outer-suburbs buyer's group, a specific professional Slack. Spend two weeks doing the unscalable version of onboarding personally, the way Stripe's founders did installs: sit with the person, watch them use it, fix what makes them hesitate. Don't touch a paid channel until that one group is fully converted and at least one person has referred you unprompted. That referral is the signal the manual phase has done its job. It's also the moment to start watching whatever channel you pick next for the first signs of decay.
