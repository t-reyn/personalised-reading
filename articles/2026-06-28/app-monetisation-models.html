🌱 Indie Income

The four ways indie apps make money — and how to pick yours

28 June 2026 · synthesised from 5 sources

Freemium, subscription, one-time, and marketplace aren't interchangeable — each fits a specific usage pattern and buyer psychology. Get this right early and you stop building for users who never pay.

The most common reason a well-built app earns nothing isn't a bad product or weak marketing — it's a monetisation model that doesn't match the way users actually interact with it. Picking the wrong model is invisible at first: you get sign-ups, decent engagement, maybe some feature requests. Then you try to charge and the conversion rate tells you something structural is off. Get the model right before you build the paywall, not after.

Model 1 — One-time purchase

The user pays once, owns the product. Best for tools where the job-to-be-done is finite: you use the app to accomplish a specific outcome, and once that's done, you're done. Think: a wedding seating planner, a tax estimator, a CV builder. The event happens, the job finishes, the user moves on — and that's fine, because you priced accordingly.

The advantage is psychological simplicity. There's no subscription fatigue, no "am I getting enough out of this each month?" anxiety. The purchase decision is discrete and the value proposition is clear. The disadvantage is that without recurring revenue, you're always selling to new customers. Your business model is CAC vs one-time LTV, with no compound effect — sustainable only if you have strong organic acquisition or a very high price point for the job.

Practical number: if your app solves a $200 problem, charge $29–$59 one-time rather than $5/month. A flat fee that feels like a no-brainer relative to the problem is easier to convert than a subscription that requires ongoing justification.

Model 2 — Subscription

Monthly or annual recurring fee. This is the engine behind most successful indie SaaS: predictable monthly recurring revenue (MRR) that compounds as you add subscribers without losing many. But it only works for tools with high return frequency — apps users open at least weekly. A gym log, a habit tracker, a finance dashboard. If users only open your app once a month, they'll cancel.

The mechanics matter:

  • Churn is existential. At 5% monthly churn, your average subscriber stays ~20 months (LTV = 20 × price). At 2% churn, they stay ~50 months. The difference in LTV is enormous — optimise for retention first, acquisition second.
  • Annual plans are worth a discount. Offering 20% off for annual billing typically doubles the share of annual subscribers. That cuts churn dramatically (annual subscribers churn 4–6× less) and gives you cash upfront.
  • Rule of thumb: subscription is viable if ≥40% of your users return weekly without prompting. If they don't, you'll fight every billing cycle.

Practical number: $5–$15/month is the sweet spot for consumer apps. Above $20/month feels like a business tool and needs to be justified as one. If your app genuinely saves users time or money every week, that's the frame — not "a small monthly fee."

Model 3 — Freemium

Core product free, premium features paid. This is seductive for indie builders because it removes the friction of asking people to pay before they've seen the value — but it introduces a harder problem: most of your users will never pay, and that's not a failure state, it's the design.

The maths of freemium conversion are brutal and worth internalising: B2C freemium typically converts 2–5% of users to paid. At 2% conversion with a $10/month plan:

  • 1,000 MAU → 20 subscribers → $200 MRR
  • 10,000 MAU → 200 subscribers → $2,000 MRR
  • 50,000 MAU → 1,000 subscribers → $10,000 MRR

To make freemium work at a meaningful revenue level without a massive user base, you either need a higher conversion rate (which means a tighter, more compelling upgrade hook) or a higher price point for premium. The best freemium gates are natural limits — storage caps, project limits, export caps — rather than feature lockouts, because users hit them through normal usage and the upgrade feels earned rather than forced.

Freemium makes most sense when free users have distribution value (they share, invite, or demonstrate your product to others) or when you need volume to validate which features people actually value before you build the paywall.

Model 4 — Marketplace / commission

You take a cut of transactions between two parties. Revenue scales with GMV (gross merchandise value) rather than with user count alone. The hardest model to bootstrap — you need liquidity on both sides — but the most defensible once it exists, because both sides have nowhere else to go.

For an indie founder, the realistic entry point is a niche marketplace where you can seed supply manually and match demand in a tightly defined category. Commission rates typically range from 5–25% depending on category and how much value the platform adds beyond the transaction.

How to choose

Ask two questions:

  1. How often does the user open the app? Daily or weekly → subscription or freemium. Once per event → one-time. Facilitating transactions → marketplace.
  2. How big is the problem? Bigger problems can bear higher prices and less friction. Small daily habits need low barriers ($3–$10/month). High-stakes events (wedding, tax, legal) can bear $50–$200 one-time.

A common mistake: launching freemium because it feels less scary than charging upfront. Freemium pushes the monetisation problem downstream and requires 50× more users to generate the same revenue as a 100% paid model at the same conversion rate. If you're not expecting viral distribution, start paid-only and add a free tier only once you understand what free users are worth to you.

The bottom line

Pick the model that fits the usage pattern, not the one that feels easiest to justify to users. A recurring-use tool with a subscription converts better than freemium if the habit is strong enough. An event-based tool with a fair one-time price converts better than a subscription that creates ongoing commitment anxiety. Get this decision right and every other part of your monetisation — pricing page, trial length, onboarding — becomes easier to optimise.
