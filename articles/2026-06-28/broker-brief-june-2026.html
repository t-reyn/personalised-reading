LRBA ban, CGT reform, rate split: the broker brief for June 2026

28 June 2026 · synthesised from 6 sources

Parliament passes negative gearing and CGT changes, APRA signals the end of SMSF residential lending, and major banks disagree on where rates go next. Here's what each shift means for your client conversations.

Three material shifts landed on brokers in June 2026, all at once. Taken together they don't cancel each other out — they reshape which client segments are growing, which are shrinking, and what conversations you'll need to have before a client can make a decision.

1 — Negative gearing and CGT reform: what passed

The Albanese government's long-flagged reforms to negative gearing and the capital gains tax (CGT) discount have cleared Parliament. The key changes for property investors:

  • Negative gearing is now limited to new residential property. Investors who buy existing (established) stock after the commencement date can no longer offset the rental loss against other income — the loss is quarantined and can only be used against future capital gains from the same asset.
  • CGT discount for investment property is reduced from 50% to 25% for properties acquired after the commencement date (assets held more than twelve months still get the discount, just the smaller one). Pre-commencement holdings are grandfathered at 50%.

What this means in practice: buying an existing investment property is now meaningfully more expensive to hold in a loss position, and the exit tax on gains is larger. The relative attractiveness of new builds shifts up sharply — they retain the full negative gearing benefit and still qualify for depreciation deductions on the structure.

Broker implication: investor clients who were weighing established vs. new are now almost always better off financially in new stock (all else equal). Expect a surge in enquiries for construction and off-the-plan loans. Know your lender panel's appetite for house-and-land packages, completion-risk loans, and progress drawdown facilities.

2 — The LRBA residential ban: a client segment is closing

Broker Daily reports that LRBAs (Limited Recourse Borrowing Arrangements) for residential property are likely to be banned. Here's what's at stake:

An LRBA is a structure that allows a Self-Managed Super Fund (SMSF) trustee to borrow money to acquire an asset — most commonly residential property. The mechanics:

  1. The SMSF borrows from a lender (a bank or non-bank).
  2. The property is held in a separate "bare trust" (also called a holding trust), not directly by the SMSF, until the loan is fully repaid.
  3. "Limited recourse" means if the SMSF defaults, the lender can only claim the property in the bare trust — the SMSF's other retirement assets are protected.
  4. Once the loan is paid off, legal title transfers from the bare trust to the SMSF.

SMSF LRBA loans carry a premium over standard investor rates — typically 50–100 basis points higher — and lenders require larger deposits (often 20–30%). It's a specialist area, and brokers who understand the structure well can add real value to SMSF trustee clients navigating it.

The regulatory concern driving the likely ban: an SMSF using an LRBA to buy one residential property is essentially concentrating a member's retirement savings into a single leveraged asset. If property prices fall significantly, the fund is doubly exposed — asset value drops while the debt stays fixed. APRA and Treasury have flagged this as an unacceptable risk to retirement savings.

Broker implication: SMSF trustees who wanted to use super to buy residential property will lose this pathway. Some may pivot to commercial property LRBAs (not yet flagged for the same ban). The advisory opportunity is in helping existing LRBA clients understand what the ban means for their current structure (typically: existing loans are grandfathered, no new residential LRBAs after the effective date).

3 — Rate split: what do you tell a client?

The major banks are publicly disagreeing on where the RBA cash rate goes from here. Westpac is forecasting the cash rate will reach 4.85% — another hike from current levels — while at least one other major bank sees rates plateauing or easing by year-end.

This matters for clients making a timing decision: should I fix now? Should I wait before refinancing? Should I lock in a rate before the next hike hits?

The honest broker answer: no one knows, and the bank economists have a poor track record calling the turning point. What you can do:

  • Model the scenarios. If rates go to 4.85%, what does the repayment look like on a $600k variable loan versus the fixed rate available today? Give clients the comparison in dollar terms, not percentage points.
  • Bias toward their risk tolerance, not your rate view. A client who is stressed at current repayments should strongly consider fixing — certainty has real value. A client with buffer and long horizon can wait.
  • Note the break cost asymmetry. If rates fall after fixing, break costs can be substantial. If rates rise, the fixed rate looks good. The asymmetry is worth naming explicitly.

Construction loans and building clients have a specific concern: rate movements during the construction period affect the ongoing loan they'll roll into. Watch for rate lock products from your lender panel — valuable for clients with long build timelines.

Briefly: Australia's potential Big 5

Reports this week suggest Australia may soon officially have a fifth major bank, as a large non-Big 4 lender reaches scale that regulators and market participants are treating as equivalent to the existing four. This changes competitive dynamics modestly — more "major bank" competition for broker flow, slightly less concentration in how lenders price and differentiate. Worth watching as it develops.

The bottom line

June 2026 is a genuinely consequential month for anyone building a mortgage broking practice. The CGT/negative gearing reform creates an immediate advisory opportunity (clients need to understand if their investment strategy still makes sense). The LRBA ban closes one specialist client segment. And the rate split means rate conversations with clients require more nuance, not less. The brokers who thrive in this environment are the ones who have done the scenarios before the client walks in — not the ones who reach for a generic "rates are uncertain" disclaimer.
