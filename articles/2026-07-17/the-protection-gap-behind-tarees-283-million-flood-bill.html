The Protection Gap Behind Taree's $283 Million Flood Bill

17 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Taree's flood claims are 92% finalised and the mud is gone — but a $8bn annual insurance tax load and a workers' comp scheme reshuffling its claims managers both point at the same underlying question: who actually carries the risk that goes unpriced.

Insurance Council CEO Andrew Hall told a Taree roundtable this week that recovery isn't resilience. The numbers behind that line: more than 14,000 claims and close to $283 million in payouts from the May 2025 floods, with 92% of those claims now finalised. Federal MP Alison Penfold's contribution was blunter — the Manning Valley and Mid North Coast still hold around 10% of Australia's flood-exposed properties, and nothing about a finalised claims book changes that exposure.

Penfold's other figure is the one worth sitting with. Roughly 30 cents of every dollar spent on insurance premiums is tax, a load she puts at $8 billion a year across the sector. That's not a claims cost — it's a structural add-on that pushes premiums higher for exactly the high-risk households least able to absorb it.

The gap between loss and cover

Taree's 92% finalisation rate reads like a success story in the numbers insurers report, but it only measures claims that were lodged. It says nothing about households in the same flood zone who went into May 2025 uninsured or underinsured — often because a tax-loaded premium priced them out before the water arrived. That distance between the true economic damage a disaster causes and the share of it insurance pays for is the insurance protection gap, and it's the number regulators and reinsurers increasingly watch instead of headline claims figures.

You already price a catastrophe allowance into a book for exactly this kind of lumpy, rare-event loss — extra margin built in because floods like Taree's don't arrive on a smooth annual schedule. That allowance only ever covers the risk an insurer underwrote. It does nothing for the risk that never got insured in the first place, which is why "claims are 92% finalised" and "the protection gap just widened" can both be true about the same event. One measures how well the industry served the customers it had; the other measures how many customers priced themselves — or got priced — out of the market entirely.

A different kind of gap, in workers' comp

ReturnToWorkSA is retendering who manages the roughly 646,000 workers on its books, worth $865 million in FY2024-25 premium, after incumbents EML and Gallagher Bassett — running the caseload since 2006 and 2013 respectively — reach the end of their contracts. New arrangements start 1 July 2027, with tender outcomes due early next year.

This is a statutory underwriting scheme: the South Australian government, not a private insurer, ultimately wears every claim cost, and this tender only decides who administers the caseload day to day. That's structurally different from group life, where the insurer you price for holds both the underwriting risk and the claims-management relationship in one place. When SA reshuffles its claims managers, the risk pool itself doesn't move — only the operational layer around it does, which is exactly why ReturnToWorkSA can promise no disruption mid-tender: continuity is a service commitment, not a risk transfer.

What to check before 2027

Two dates carry the real signal. The tender outcome, due early 2027, will show whether claims-management competition in a monopoly-underwritten scheme moves cost or just moves administration. And on the flood side, the figure that matters isn't the 92% finalisation rate — it's whether Disaster Ready Fund mitigation spend in the Manning Valley shifts that 10% flood-exposure share before the next event, rather than after another roundtable counts the claims.
