Broking's Next Growth Line Isn't Home Loans Anymore

10 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

Three Broker Daily stories published the same day describe one shift: SME clients are borrowing to survive rather than grow, aggregators are racing into commercial and SMSF lending to catch them, and none of it works on top of a dirty CRM.

On the same day AFG opened its white-label suite to commercial and SMSF borrowers, Broker Daily reported that SME clients had swung from borrowing to grow into borrowing just to survive — and separately, that most brokers can't see their own client base clearly enough in their CRM to catch either shift.

Cash flow, not growth, is driving demand

Banjo Loans' quarterly barometer shows average SME loan size down 20% in Q4 FY26, a 5% annual decline, while borrower-initiated loan cancellations — a proxy for lenders being shopped around and dropped — surged 814%. Among businesses with $10 million-plus revenue, average loan size fell 37%. Banjo CEO Guy Callaghan put it bluntly: "SMEs are doing it tough right now... not to fund growth, but simply to keep the lights on." Wage costs, margin compression, the Payday Super rollout, rate uncertainty and softer consumer spending — hospitality and cafes worst hit — are the named pressures. MC Finance Group's Matthew Chik reports the same shift toward "strengthening their liquidity."

It isn't universal. Soren Financial's Mansour Soltani still sees solid demand from cash-flow-healthy construction, manufacturing and logistics firms funding property, equipment and acquisitions — and Western Sydney's commercial lending market grew 11.1% year-on-year regardless. The read isn't "SMEs stopped borrowing" — it's that the client sitting across the desk could be either a defensive working-capital case or a selective growth case, and a broker who defaults to one script for both is going to misread the deal.

Product range just got wider, right on cue

AFG launched its AFGHL Options expansion into commercial (full-doc and low-doc) and SMSF lending — the latter secured by commercial property only — in Sydney on 9 July, alongside Pepper Money reporting roughly 60% year-on-year growth across its own commercial and SMSF books. AFG's Hayden Cush framed the timing: "The next stage of growth for brokers is moving beyond residential loans." That timing isn't coincidental — residential property borrowing through an SMSF limited recourse borrowing arrangement (an LRBA lets a fund borrow, via a bare trust, to buy a single asset) closes to new borrowers from 10 August 2026. Brokers with SMSF clients who'd normally have gone the residential LRBA route now have eight weeks to redirect them toward commercial-secured SMSF lending or a different structure entirely, before the option disappears.

None of it works on top of a dirty CRM

The catch, per broker coach Jason Back of Broker Essentials: most brokerages use only 20-40% of their CRM's actual capability, and "the data in a lot of CRMs is really dirty" — missing emails, dates of birth, occupations and business details, plus old leads nobody's reviewed. Back's line lands hard against the AFG opportunity specifically: "technology without adoption is effectively just wasting money." His fix is unglamorous — export the client list to CSV and audit it, learn what the existing CRM already does before buying a new tool, pilot small before rolling out to the whole book, and document the process so it survives beyond the principal's own head.

The audit before the opportunity

Put the three together and the sequence is obvious: you can't sell a commercial or SMSF product to a client whose SMSF status or business turnover isn't accurately recorded, and you can't tell a defensive borrower from a growth borrower off gut feel once your pipeline gets past a couple of dozen files. Before chasing the AFG-style opportunity, run Back's CSV audit and check how many existing clients have accurate SMSF or business-structure fields populated at all — that's the fastest way to find out whether the new white-label products are actually reachable from your current book, or just theoretical.
