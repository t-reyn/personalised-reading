What AI Is Actually Doing Inside Australian Life Insurers

10 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

The Actuaries Institute's Life Insurance Data and AI Working Group named six live AI use cases in Australian life insurers — and none of them makes a pricing or reserving call. A second Institute session the same fortnight explains why that gap matters.

At the Actuaries Institute's Life Insurance Appointed Actuaries forum in May, the Life Insurance Data and AI Working Group didn't present a roadmap — it presented six things AI is already doing inside Australian insurers, and not one of them is a pricing, reserving or underwriting decision.

The six jobs AI already has

Working group lead Tim Lam listed them plainly: medical evidence summarisation, claims case summarisation, financial advice assistance, pre-assessment triaging, vulnerable customer identification, and corporate quote generation. Every one is a task that already existed — AI is doing it faster and more consistently, not inventing a new capability. Lam's own framing: "The efficiency benefits of AI are easy to see... yet for all its promise, AI presently appears to help us do existing tasks better rather than paving a new way forward." The working group flagged agentic AI — systems that chain several steps together rather than complete one bounded task — as the actual next frontier, still to arrive.

The forum's other sessions read as the reason that gap exists. APRA's Colette Reid spoke on longevity capital requirements and pushed for industry-led solutions over prescriptive rules. The Carpe Diem working group (Jas Singh and Kent Hopper) walked through why new business volumes and portfolio performance are stuck: shrinking direct and bancassurance channels, a falling adviser headcount, Life Insurance Framework commission caps, awkward lump-sum TPD product design, and lingering Royal Commission reputational drag. None of that is a task AI can summarise its way out of.

Frameworks are the part under strain

Two days later, the Institute's All Actuaries Summit ran a plenary explicitly asking whether risk frameworks can keep pace with what they're pricing. Insurance Council of Australia's Alix Pearce put a number on one version of the problem: Australia carries a $12 billion insurance protection gap — the difference between what a disaster actually costs and what's covered — against $327 billion in uninsured extreme-weather losses globally in a single year. Her point cuts against the obvious fix: "Higher premiums don't solve the protection gap. They widen it," because pricing risk away from a community just pushes more of it uninsured. Closing the gap needs resilience investment and new risk-spreading structures, not a bigger loading.

Finity's Rade Musulin made a related point about geopolitical risk: catastrophe models built on historical loss data miss how a war or a trade shock amplifies costs through labour shortages and materials spikes — a category of loss that doesn't show up until it already has. See. Be. Consulting's Corey Barker went further on technology risk specifically: "risk cycles are compressing," with exposures now shifting in months rather than years, which makes a static exclusion clause obsolete faster than an insurer can redraft it. His recommendation was to move from broad blanket exclusions toward structured insurability — understanding what a risk actually depends on, rather than carving it out wholesale.

Where the two sessions meet

Put the forum and the plenary side by side and they're describing the same actuary from two directions. AI is already competent at the bounded, repeatable half of the job — reading a file, triaging a claim, drafting a quote. The half under genuine strain is judgment: deciding when a risk category itself needs rebuilding rather than re-pricing, which is exactly what Reid, Pearce, Musulin and Barker were each arguing in their own domain. Daniel Ch'ng's write-up of the appointed actuaries forum makes the same case directly — the job is shifting from running the number to challenging the framework the number sits inside, and doing it with enough technical discipline that the challenge lands.

The audit worth doing this month

None of the six AI use cases Lam listed needs agentic AI to matter today. If your practice hasn't mapped which of medical evidence review, claims summarisation, triage or quote drafting AI already touches in your workflow, that's a smaller, more immediate task than waiting for the "next frontier" to force the question — and it's the honest starting point for figuring out which parts of the job are still yours.
