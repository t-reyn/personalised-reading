Six Fixes Cover 96% Of Why Sites Fail Accessibility

23 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

WebAIM's 2026 scan of a million homepages found 95.9% fail accessibility — but the same six fixable problems, led by low-contrast text, cause 96% of it. What each one actually requires, and when a dropdown is one of them.

WebAIM scanned the home pages of the world's top one million websites in February 2026 and found detectable accessibility failures on 95.9% of them — an average of 56.1 barriers per page, up 10.1% on last year's 51. It's the eighth straight year the same study has run, and the pass rate keeps moving the wrong way. The useful part isn't the headline number, though. It's that six recurring, fixable problems account for 96% of everything the scan flags. If you're shipping your own products, those six are worth knowing by name, because they aren't really six problems — they're two mechanisms wearing six labels.

Six things, not a thousand things

In order: low-contrast text (83.9% of pages), missing image alt text (53.1%), missing form input labels (51%), empty links (46.3%), empty buttons (30.6%), and a missing page-language tag (13.5%). Four of those six are the same failure repeated on four different elements: something a screen reader needs to announce simply isn't there in the code, even though the page looks fine on screen. One is a colour-maths problem. One is a single missing HTML attribute. And because WebAIM's scan is automated, testing only what a script can verify against WCAG 2.2, the real failure rate — judged by someone actually using a screen reader — runs higher than 95.9%, not lower.

The number you already half-know

You've already met contrast as a design tool: making the important thing pop against the page. WCAG turns that into a hard pass/fail number instead of a design choice. Success Criterion 1.4.3 requires a 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text — anything under 18 point, or under 14 point bold — and 3:1 for text at or above that size. In CSS terms that threshold sits around 24px for regular weight and 18.5px bold, so your body copy needs the stricter ratio and your large stat callouts and headings get the easier one. A lot of the grey-on-white body text that looks tasteful in a mockup measures out around 3:1–4:1 against white — comfortably in the zone that fails, and the single biggest reason 83.9% of pages get flagged. Run your palette through a free contrast checker before you ship it, not after.

If it's not in the text, it doesn't exist

A screen reader reads what's in the DOM — element text and ARIA labels — not what's on screen. An icon-only button with no accessible name is, to both a screen reader and to WebAIM's scanner, an empty button: 30.6% of pages have at least one. An <img> with no alt attribute is invisible cargo, which is most of that 53.1%. A form input with a placeholder but no <label> looks complete visually and says nothing when it gets focus — that's the 51%. A link that only wraps a bare icon, or says "click here" with no context, is the 46.3%. All four share the same fix: name the thing in code, not just in the layout. Before your next release, tab through your own form with the mouse put away — anything that reads out as "button" or "link" with no further detail is on your list.

The dropdown is rarely the right default

Nielsen Norman Group's research adds a practical rule most solo builders skip: dropdowns are a middling choice most of the time, not a safe default. Under about seven options, an exposed set of radio buttons beats a dropdown on both speed and accessibility — major design systems converge on similar thresholds, from Carbon's 3 to Material's 6 to USWDS's 7. Above roughly 15 options, a plain dropdown breaks down from scrolling and fine-motor-control strain, and the fix is a searchable combobox, not a longer list. GOV.UK's own research found enough friction — users unsure whether an item was focused or selected, unsure how much further there was to scroll — that its public-facing services now treat a plain dropdown as a last resort. If SeatFlow asks for a table size or Shojin asks to pick an exercise from a long list, that choice of input is doing as much accessibility work as any colour or label ever will.

Test it with the screen reader on, not just the mouse away

Turn on your phone's built-in screen reader — VoiceOver on iPhone, TalkBack on Android — for sixty seconds on whichever product you last shipped a form to. Every button, link and field it can't name out loud is a line item you can fix in minutes, and it's a truer test than eyeballing a Figma file, because it catches exactly the four failures that show up on half the web.
