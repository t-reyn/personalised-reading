What IAG's RAC Remedy Offer Tells You About Merger Review

12 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

IAG has tabled a competitiveness framework and a repair-facility commitment to get its $1.35 billion RAC Insurance deal past the ACCC. The mechanics of a merger remedy, and why competition regulators care about your combined ratio, in one live case study.

IAG's $1.35 billion bid for WA's RAC Insurance has hit exactly the wall you'd expect: the ACCC isn't blocking the deal outright, it's negotiating the terms IAG gets to keep it. This month IAG tabled a formal remedy offer, and the regulator has given the market until 22 July to say whether it's enough. That back-and-forth is a live worked example of a mechanism every actuary working in general insurance should understand, because it changes the profitability assumptions behind a merger, not just its approval odds.

What a merger remedy actually is

When a competition regulator worries a deal will let the combined business raise prices or degrade service without fear of losing customers, it has three options: block the deal, wave it through, or accept a remedy — a binding commitment the acquirer makes to preserve competitive pressure. Remedies split into two families. Structural remedies change ownership: sell off an overlapping business unit, divest a brand. Behavioural remedies leave ownership intact but constrain conduct going forward. IAG's offer is squarely behavioural: a "competitiveness framework" committing IAG's WA products to stay competitively priced, plus a pledge not to own or control smash-repair facilities in the state (with limited exceptions). No divestment, no structural change — just enforceable promises about how the merged entity will behave.

Why behavioural remedies are the harder sell

Structural remedies are easy for a regulator to verify: either the business was sold or it wasn't. Behavioural remedies require ongoing monitoring — is the pricing framework actually holding prices down, or just providing cover for a slower drift upward that's hard to prove in any single quarter? That's precisely why the ACCC's follow-up questionnaire to the market, due back by 22 July, is probing three specific mechanisms: distribution agreements, smash-repair services, and repair-facility ownership. Each is a channel through which a merged IAG-RAC could quietly extract more margin than the headline pricing commitment prevents — for instance, steering claims toward owned repairers even without owning them outright, through preferred-supplier arrangements the remedy doesn't obviously cover.

The number regulators are actually protecting

The combined ratio — claims plus expenses as a share of premium — is the practical translation of "market power" into a P&L line. Fewer credible competitors in WA general insurance means less pressure on IAG-RAC to hold that ratio down to win business, because customers have fewer places to go if pricing or claims handling drifts worse. That's the entire economic logic behind the ACCC's scrutiny: it isn't policing IAG's intentions, it's protecting the competitive pressure that keeps a combined ratio honest. A remedy that looks watertight on price but leaves claims-handling and repair-network incentives loose is exactly the gap a sharp competitor exploits and a sharp regulator interrogates — which is why the ACCC's questionnaire goes well past the headline pricing commitment IAG led with.

What to watch

The ACCC's feedback deadline of 22 July is the next real checkpoint — expect either a strengthened remedy package or a formal statement of concerns shortly after. For anyone pricing or reserving in general insurance, the more durable lesson travels beyond this one deal: when a merger clears on a behavioural remedy rather than a divestment, the regulator is betting that a written commitment can substitute for market structure. Worth tracking whether IAG's WA loss ratios and combined ratio actually hold steady against the rest of its book once the deal completes — that's the real-world test of whether the remedy did its job.
