The Comeback Ratio's 0.8–1.3 Sweet Spot Is Partly a Mathematical Illusion

21 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

The acute:chronic workload ratio is the standard tool for pacing a return to sport, but the maths behind its 0.8–1.3 sweet spot also makes it look more predictive than it really is, and that changes how you should use it.

If you're working out how fast to ramp back into full training after months out, sports science already has a number for it: the acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR), your last 7 days of training load divided by your rolling 28-day average. Land between 0.8 and 1.3 and you're in the accepted sweet spot; drift past 1.5, and the research behind the ratio puts you at two to four times the injury risk of someone who built load up gradually. It's the most-cited rule in return-to-play planning, and it has a flaw baked directly into its own arithmetic that should change how you use it.

Where the number comes from

The ratio traces back to Tim Gabbett, an Australian sports scientist. His 2016 analysis of rugby league, cricket and AFL squads, cohorts running from 28 to a couple of hundred athletes each, found that players who spiked above a 1.5 ratio carried markedly higher soft-tissue injury rates than teammates who built load steadily. It's why strength and conditioning staff across professional and amateur sport now track weekly load against a rolling month, rather than just counting minutes or kilometres in isolation.

The maths problem baked in

Here's the catch: your acute week is also a quarter of the chronic average it's being measured against. The two figures aren't independent; the yardstick is partly made of the thing being measured. That overlap inflates how predictive the ratio looks on paper. Tested properly, ACWR's real discrimination between who gets injured and who doesn't sits around an AUC of 0.55 to 0.65, well short of the roughly 0.80 usually considered clinically useful for an individual decision. Several of the original findings haven't replicated cleanly either, and critics point out that acute load or chronic load alone often predicts about as well as the ratio does. The proposed fix, an exponentially weighted moving average that removes the mathematical overlap, produces different numbers again depending on how it's calculated, so even the fix isn't settled science.

What that means for your comeback

None of that makes the ratio useless; it means treating it as a guardrail rather than a formula to trust blindly. Once you've cleared the real functional bar — a hop test battery scored for limb symmetry, not a date circled on the calendar — ACWR is what should govern how fast training minutes climb afterwards, not a single number deciding whether you're fit to play. Work out your current chronic load first: average weekly training minutes across the last month, close to zero if you've been genuinely sidelined. From there, don't let this week's return jump more than 30 to 50% past that average. Going from near-zero straight to a full 90-minute basketball session isn't a 1.3 ratio; it's an undefined, effectively infinite one, and it's exactly the spike pattern the research flags.

Where zone 2 fits back in

This is also where zone 2 training earns its keep as more than a base-building idea: it's how you accumulate genuine chronic load again without the joint- and soft-tissue-loading spikes of intervals or full-contact sessions. Rebuild three to four weeks of steady, conversational-pace minutes first, hold the ratio inside roughly 0.8 to 1.3 as that base grows, and only then start layering in the change-of-direction and contact work basketball genuinely demands. Skip straight to game intensity and you're not testing your ankle against a number at all; you're testing it against the exact spike Gabbett's data flags as high-risk.

The number worth writing down this week

Track total training minutes for the next four weeks, not just the sessions that feel hard: easy zone 2 work counts toward chronic load too. Treat 1.3 as a caution line rather than a hard ceiling, and treat any single week's ratio as noise on its own. The trend across a rolling month is the part worth trusting.
