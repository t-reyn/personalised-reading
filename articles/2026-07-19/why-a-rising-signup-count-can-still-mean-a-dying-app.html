Why a Rising Signup Count Can Still Mean a Dying App

19 July 2026 · foundations piece

A weekly signup count can only go up, but the cohort retention curve — and the "smile" shape at the tail of it — is what actually tells you whether anyone's still opening the app a month on.

Every chart an indie builder is tempted to screenshot only moves one way: total signups, cumulative downloads, all-time revenue. Add a user this week and the line ticks up; lose one who quietly stopped opening the app, and the line doesn't even notice. A chart that's mathematically incapable of falling can't tell you whether the product works. It can only tell you whether the top of the funnel does.

The chart that's allowed to fall

A cohort retention curve asks a narrower question: of the people who signed up in a given week, what share are still active — not "ever opened it once," but active — a day later, a week later, a month later, three months later? Group 200 people who joined in the same week into one cohort, and track that same 200 forward, never swapping in anyone new. A typical shape: 62% open the app the next day, 38% are still there at day 7, 21% at day 30, and by day 90 it settles near 13%. That steep early drop is normal; most signups are curious, not committed, and a chunk of any cohort was never going to stay no matter what got built.

What flat is proving

The number that matters isn't the day-30 figure by itself, it's whether the curve keeps sliding toward zero or finds a floor. A curve that plateaus, holding somewhere near 12–14% out past day 90, has found its core: the users for whom the app became a genuine habit rather than a one-off download. A curve still falling at day 90 at the same rate it was falling at day 10 means there's no habit loop yet, whatever the weekly signup number sitting on top of it looks like. The LTV maths behind any CAC calculation assumes a churn rate; this is where that number comes from — the flat part of a real cohort curve, not a guess pulled from three months of noisy data.

The smile: when the curve turns back up

Look far enough out and a healthy consumer app's curve does something stranger than flatten: it dips, plateaus, then edges up again, months later. Take the earlier cohort: 13% still active at day 90. Watch that same 200 people out to day 180 and it's not unusual to see the figure tick up to 15–16%, not because anyone new joined the cohort, but because some of the 87% who'd gone quiet came back. That uptick is resurrection: users who lapsed for weeks, through travel, a busy quarter, an abandoned New Year resolution, returning off a push notification, a feature release, or just remembering the app exists. A cohort's active count isn't strictly one-way; someone counted as churned in month two can un-churn in month five. Treat every dormant user as gone for good and you'll undercount lifetime value, and overspend acquiring replacements for people who were already on their way back.

Where product taste shows up

Two apps can run identical acquisition funnels and still land on completely different plateau heights: one flattens at 4%, the other at 18%, same signup cost, same feature list on paper. That gap is where product taste, the accumulated judgement calls on what to cut and refine rather than just ship, gets marked. A feature checklist can be copied in a weekend. A curve that settles at 18% instead of 4% took months of deciding what not to build.

Pull your own curve before you build anything else

Building this needs nothing more than a spreadsheet. List every user with their signup date, then for day 1, day 7 and day 30 mark whether they opened the app relative to their own signup date, not the calendar date, which is the mistake that makes most homemade cohort charts meaningless. If your oldest cohort hasn't hit 30 days yet, that's fine for now. The number worth watching this month is simpler: whether last month's cohort is still sliding at day 30, or whether it's found a floor.
