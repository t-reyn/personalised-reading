Broker Brief: End of FY26 — Tax Reform Fallout, APRA's Presale Shakeup, SME Arrears

29 June 2026 · synthesised from 5 sources

Three developments every AU broker needs to track as the financial year closes: lenders revising servicing calculators after Labor's investor tax reforms, APRA proposing new presale rules for development finance, and SME payment arrears on the rise.

1. Lenders revising servicing policies after tax reforms

Labor's investor property tax changes — limiting negative gearing deductions to new properties from 1 July 2026, and reducing the CGT discount from 50% to 25% for assets acquired after that date — have cleared parliament. Multiple lenders have already begun updating their serviceability calculators in response.

The mechanism is straightforward: negative gearing lets investors offset rental losses against other income, reducing taxable income and improving assessed cash flow. With that deduction now restricted to new builds for future purchases, an investor buying an established property after 1 July no longer gets the full rental loss offset. Lenders who model after-tax cash flows (rather than gross rental income) need to recalibrate — and several have done so ahead of the July 1 effective date.

What it means for your clients: An existing investor who passed serviceability easily in FY25 may need to be re-run through current calculators before they can add to their portfolio. Run a fresh serviceability check on any investor client who is considering a purchase — don't assume last year's approval implies the same capacity today. New-build purchases are carved out; push clients interested in established property to understand the after-tax cash flow difference before committing.

2. APRA proposes shake-up of presale requirements

APRA has flagged a proposed revision to presale coverage requirements for property development construction loans. Background: lenders currently require developers to have a minimum proportion of units under signed off-the-plan contracts (typically 100% coverage, sometimes 80%) before they will approve or drawdown a construction facility. The rationale is credit risk — presales signal market demand and provide a de facto cash flow backstop. The downside is that tight presale thresholds act as a brake on housing supply, particularly in a market where buyers are reluctant to commit off-the-plan.

APRA's proposed changes would allow regulated lenders to apply lower presale thresholds where developers can demonstrate alternative risk mitigants — stronger equity, experienced developers, or pre-committed institutional buyers. The consultation is ongoing, but several non-bank lenders have already been operating below traditional thresholds, and this proposal would bring major ADIs closer to non-bank practice.

What it means for your clients: If you work with developer clients or investors in new builds, this is worth watching. Loosened presale requirements could increase the pipeline of new supply — supporting the government's housing targets — but also introduces more construction risk into lender portfolios. In the short term, it may open up development finance conversations with clients who previously couldn't attract a major bank.

3. SME payment arrears climbing — proactive broking the response

Broker Daily data points to rising payment arrears among small business clients. Higher base rates, persistent input cost pressures, and slower consumer spending are squeezing SME cash flows — particularly in hospitality, retail, and trade services. Asset-based lending (equipment finance, debtor finance) is gaining traction as an alternative to traditional term loans for businesses that need liquidity without adding to secured debt.

The industry call is for proactive outreach rather than waiting for clients to come to you in distress. The brokers who are winning SME business right now are running quarterly cash flow conversations — leading with the client's financial position, not with a rate offer. A client whose business is under pressure doesn't need a better rate; they need a refinance structure or working capital solution that matches their actual cash cycle.

Business confidence has rebounded in recent surveys, suggesting the squeeze is felt unevenly — some sectors are under real pressure while others (construction, energy, professional services) are seeing solid demand. Segment your SME book before you reach out.

End-of-year action checklist

  • Investor clients: Re-run serviceability through current lender calculators before any FY27 purchase — new CGT and negative gearing rules effective 1 July.
  • Off-the-plan buyers: Watch the APRA presale consultation — new supply pipeline could improve stock availability in 12–18 months.
  • SME clients: Proactive cash flow call before July — identify who might need a refinance or working capital facility before they're in arrears.
  • Rate reviews: End of FY is a natural trigger for a home loan review conversation. The RBA cut cycle has room to run; borrowers on high standard variable rates have not all been repriced.
