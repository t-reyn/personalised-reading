Your First 10 Users: The Only Number That Matters at Launch

29 June 2026 · synthesised from 4 sources

Getting early users for a consumer app is less about marketing and more about manual, targeted outreach — and in an AI world, taste is the skill that separates winners.

The number nobody talks about

Most indie builders obsess over launch day metrics — App Store rankings, Product Hunt upvotes, social impressions. None of that matters until you have ten real users who actually use the thing. Ten is the number that tells you whether the core idea is working. Everything before ten is still product validation; everything after is distribution.

YC visiting partner Max Kolysh, who coaches early-stage founders, puts it plainly: the only tactic that works at zero is going where your buyer actually spends their time and showing up there in person or as close to it as you can. No ad budget, no SEO playbook — direct, manual reach.

Show up where they live

For a gym-tracking app the target user is already online — in subreddits like r/fitness and r/weightroom, in Facebook groups for local gyms and powerlifting clubs, on Discord servers for strength athletes. For a wedding seating tool the buyer is a bride or a wedding planner, and they congregate in Facebook groups ("Sydney Brides 2026", vendor recommendation threads), on Pinterest boards, and in WhatsApp threads their celebrant started. The audience is findable; the question is whether you'll do the work to be present there before you have a product people are sharing.

Kolysh's insight is that showing up in person wins — whether that's a local gym, a wedding expo, or a DM to someone who just posted "planning my seating chart, help". Doing it yourself is your biggest advantage right now. You understand the problem better than anyone you could hire to do outreach, and early users can tell.

Pick one and burn the boats

YC general partner Jon Xu coaches founders who are juggling multiple ideas simultaneously. His advice: pick one, go deep enough to practically run your early customers' lives, and resist the urge to hedge. The hedge instinct — maintaining two or three projects in parallel "just in case" — is the most common early-stage mistake. Each project gets a diluted version of your attention, and none of them gets the depth of insight that turns a good idea into a product people recommend unprompted.

If you're shipping three products simultaneously, the honest question is: which one are you going to spend 30 days doing nothing but talking to users? That one becomes real. The others stay in development-mode limbo.

Taste: the new scarcest skill

Andrew Ambrosino, who led the OpenAI Codex team, made a point that keeps getting sharper as AI improves: execution is being commoditised. A solo builder today can ship what used to take a team of five. That means the bottleneck is no longer "can I build it?" — it's "do I know what good looks like for this specific user?"

Taste — the ability to recognise and make decisions about quality before you get user feedback — is becoming the scarcest skill in product work. Taste comes from spending time with users, from being a genuine power user of your own product, and from consuming enough great products in adjacent spaces to internalise what quality feels like. It's not innate; it's cultivated.

The implication for an AI-assisted builder: the code is the easy part now. The hard part is having a strong opinion about what the onboarding should feel like, why this screen layout is confusing, and what the right default is for a new user who hasn't yet found their footing.

Mark Pincus on why consumer is open again

Zynga founder Mark Pincus, who built Words With Friends and FarmVille and saw eight of ten major launches become hits, argues this is one of the best moments in history to build consumer products. AI lowers the production cost of software dramatically — but it doesn't lower the cost of understanding users. The winners will be builders who combine cheap execution with hard-won user insight.

His pattern: the hit products at Zynga weren't the most technically impressive. They were the ones where the team understood one specific emotional job — the 15-minute break, the thing you do with friends on your phone — better than anyone else.

The five things to do this week

  1. Name your ten: Write down ten specific real people who fit your ideal user — gym regulars you know, people planning weddings in your circle, colleagues who'd use your tool. Start there, not with a segment.
  2. Go to them: Message those ten individually. Not a group blast — individual, personalised, asking for 20 minutes to watch them try the product.
  3. Record every session: Take notes or record (with permission). You're looking for the moment they pause, backtrack, or skip something.
  4. Solve one problem per week: Don't collect ten sessions and then rebuild. Fix the biggest friction immediately, then go back to the same users.
  5. Ask for a referral: At the end of every session: "Who else do you know who has this problem?" Your first 10 become your path to 100.

Revenue is a lagging indicator. Referrals from real users are the leading one. The first referral you get — unsolicited — is the signal that you've found something real.
