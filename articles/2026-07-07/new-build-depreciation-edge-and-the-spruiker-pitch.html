The New-Build Tax Edge Is Real — So Is the Sales Pitch Riding On It

7 July 2026 · synthesised from 1 source

A buyer's agency is marketing new builds hard off last week's negative gearing news, with a July 2027 date attached. The underlying tax edge checks out — the pitch built on top of it needs a harder look.

A press release doing the rounds this week puts a number on something you already half-knew: from July 2027, negative gearing quarantines on established property, new builds keep the full benefit, and at least one buyer's agency is now telling clients this is "the property play" of the year. The date is new information worth banking. The framing around it deserves more scrutiny than the quote it came with.

The reform recap, in one paragraph

You've seen the mechanics before: negative gearing on established residential property gets quarantined against other income (losses can only offset future capital gains on the same asset), the CGT discount on newly acquired investment property drops from 50% to 25%, and new builds are carved out of both changes. What's new this week is the date attached to it: the government has confirmed July 2027 as commencement, and a spruiker's pitch has arrived right on schedule.

Why new builds actually get more, not just longer

The tax edge on new builds isn't only the negative gearing carve-out. It compounds with a rule that's already been law since 2017: only the original owner of a new residential property can claim depreciation on the plant and equipment inside it (appliances, carpet, hot water systems); buy established stock and that deduction is gone before you settle. Layer on top of that the capital works deduction under Division 43: 2.5% of the building's construction cost, claimable every year for up to 40 years. A new build with a $380,000 construction cost (excluding land) throws off roughly $9,500 a year in capital works deductions alone, on a schedule an established property built decades ago has usually already run down to zero. None of that requires a spruiker to be true — it's existing tax law, and from July 2027 it stacks with the negative gearing change too.

The pitch, and the person selling it

Where the press release stops being useful is the leap from "the numbers favour new builds" to "buy this specific project now." The quote — from the CEO of a buyer's agency that sources new-build stock for clients — calls the accommodation crisis and the tax change together "clearly the property play today." That's a business telling you to buy the exact product it sells, and it's worth naming the incentive plainly: developers commonly pay a referral commission to the agent who brings them a buyer, often worth several percent of the purchase price, and it isn't always volunteered upfront. That doesn't make new builds a bad idea — the depreciation maths above is real regardless of who's telling you about it. It means a recommendation that happens to align with someone's commission needs the same scrutiny you'd give any advice where the adviser gets paid more for one answer than the other.

The one question worth asking

Before acting on any new-build pitch, ask the agent directly whether they're paid by the developer for this specific project, and how much. A straight answer doesn't disqualify the deal; a dodge should. Then run your own numbers rather than borrowing theirs: get a quantity surveyor's depreciation schedule quoted for the actual property (not a generic industry estimate), check the new-build premium against comparable established stock in the same suburb, and factor in that off-the-plan and newly built stock has historically shown weaker capital growth than established property in the same location, tax perks aside. The depreciation and negative-gearing maths genuinely favours new builds from July 2027. Whether a specific project is a good investment is a separate question, and it's the one the pitch is designed to skip past.
