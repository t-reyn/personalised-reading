The Numbers That Actually Clear You to Play Again

7 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

'Ease back in' isn't a return-to-play criterion. Sports medicine has a harder one: the limb symmetry index and a battery of hop tests — and even a passing score has a catch worth knowing.

Most return-to-play advice is vibes: "listen to your body," "ease back in gently." Sports medicine runs on a harder number, the limb symmetry index, backed by a battery of hop tests, and it's worth knowing before your ankle, not your enthusiasm, decides you're ready for basketball again.

Past the grading and the wobble board

Once you've worked through the sprain grading, settled the swelling, and rebuilt balance and joint position sense on a wobble board or single-leg stance, the question changes. It stops being "does it hurt" and becomes "can the injured side do what the healthy one does." That's a measurable question, not a feeling one.

The limb symmetry index

The limb symmetry index (LSI) is a simple ratio: (affected limb score ÷ unaffected limb score) × 100. Score a single-leg hop for distance on both sides and you get a percentage, cleared against your own healthy leg, not a population average. Rehab protocols commonly treat 90% as the return-to-sport line, with a passing band usually cited between 80% and 95%.

Here's the catch worth knowing before you treat 90% as a green light: research following ACL reconstructions found that more than half the athletes who hit a 90% LSI at six months still weren't back to their own pre-injury performance level. The unaffected leg can quietly lose strength during rehab too, from disuse or compensating for the injured side, which inflates the ratio without either leg being where it used to be. A passing score against today's healthy leg isn't the same as a passing score against the leg you had before you got hurt.

Why one hop test isn't enough

Return-to-play protocols use a battery: the single hop for distance, the triple crossover hop, side hops in 30 seconds, a vertical hop for height, and a repeated vertical hop over 10 seconds, because each one loads the ankle and the rest of the chain differently. Horizontal hops test straight-line power and deceleration; vertical hops load the joint differently again and tend to expose asymmetries the horizontal tests miss. Side hops carry their own rough benchmark, 55-plus reps in 30 seconds for men and 43-plus for women, run at least three times each to rule out a learning effect rather than a real number. Pass one test and fail another, and the one you failed is the one that matters.

Rebuilding the aerobic side, not just the ankle

Once the hop numbers clear, the temptation is to go straight back to game speed. The same logic that makes zone 2 the right starting point for building an aerobic base applies directly here — low, sustained intensity lets tendon and the still-remodelling ankle tissue adapt to repeated load before you ask them to handle change-of-direction or sprint forces. Start any return-to-run block in zone 2, not at your old half-marathon pace, and let the hop numbers, not the calendar, decide when you progress past it.

What clears you, not what convinces you

Get single-leg hop numbers taken properly. A physio with a stopwatch and a tape measure is enough; you don't need a lab. Don't clear yourself on one good test — run the full battery and use the lowest score, not the best one. And treat a 90% LSI as a floor to keep training past, not a finish line, especially against a healthy leg that may have quietly lost some of its own capacity while you were favouring it.
