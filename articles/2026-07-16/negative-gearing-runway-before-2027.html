Negative Gearing Doesn't Bite Until 1 July 2027 — Fix Your Structure Before Then

16 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

The reform passed a fortnight ago, but the rules that actually change your tax bill don't start until 1 July 2027. That gap is the best window to get title ownership and trust decisions right before the grandfathering window closes.

The negative gearing and CGT reform that cleared Parliament a fortnight ago doesn't change anyone's tax bill until 1 July 2027. New-build purchases and anything you already hold stay exempt from the bite until then. That eighteen-month runway is the best window most property investors will get to fix a structure they set up without thinking about it, before the settings that reward getting it wrong disappear.

Quick recap, since this builds on an earlier piece. From commencement, negative gearing on an established property no longer offsets your salary; the loss can only be carried against future capital gains on that same asset. The CGT discount on anything acquired after that date drops from 50% to 25%. New builds keep the full negative gearing benefit, and pre-commencement holdings are grandfathered at the old 50% discount. None of that changes today. What changes today is how much runway you have to get two boring, structural decisions right while the old rules still apply to what you buy.

Whose name is on the title matters more than it used to

Buy with a partner or a family member and you'll default into one of two structures: joint tenancy or tenancy in common. Under joint tenancy, ownership is forced to an equal share between owners by law, and income and losses follow that ownership share — so a joint-tenant couple splits a negatively-geared loss 50/50 regardless of who earns more. Tenancy in common lets owners hold unequal, specified shares instead, and the loss follows whatever split you choose. Say you earn $180k and your partner earns $45k: as joint tenants you each claim half the deduction, wasting a chunk of it at your partner's lower marginal rate. As tenants in common with a 90/10 split in your favour, 90% of the loss lands against your higher rate instead, a meaningfully bigger after-tax outcome for the identical property, identical loan, identical rent. It's a form filled out with a conveyancer, not a restructure, which is exactly why it's worth getting right on the next purchase rather than fixed later.

The trust temptation, and its catch

A discretionary trust is the other structure that comes up in this conversation, usually pitched on asset protection: because the trustee legally owns the property rather than you personally, it's harder for a creditor or a future claim to reach it. That protection is real. What often gets missed is what happens to a loss inside the structure: a trust's losses are trapped inside the trust and carried forward, usable only against the trust's own future income, and cannot flow through to offset a beneficiary's personal salary the way a directly-held negatively-geared property can. Put a heavily-geared property into a trust expecting the same tax outcome as personal ownership, and the deduction you were counting on simply doesn't show up on your return this year. It sits inside the trust, waiting for the trust to earn enough of its own income to use it. For a portfolio built on negative gearing early and consolidating into trusts later, that sequencing, geared personally first, trust-held once cash-flow positive, is the detail worth getting right from the start, not after the first disappointing tax return.

None of this is a reason to rush a purchase. With the ASX 200 sitting at 8,841.1 (+0.4%, as of 6:51am AEST today) near cycle highs, the opportunity cost of parking capital in a badly-structured property is a real, comparable number, not an abstract one, while equities keep compounding in the background.

What to lock in before the runway shortens

If a purchase with a partner or family member is on the table before 1 July 2027, decide the ownership split — joint tenants or tenants in common, and at what percentage — before you get to settlement, because it's far cheaper to specify on the title than to transfer it later and trigger stamp duty and CGT on your own restructure. And if a trust is part of the plan, model the year-one cash flow assuming the negative gearing deduction stays trapped, not assuming it behaves like personal ownership. Get both right on the next purchase, and the 2027 deadline becomes background noise instead of a scramble.
