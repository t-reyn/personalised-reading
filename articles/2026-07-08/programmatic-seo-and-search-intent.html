Why Some Indie Apps Rank for Thousands of Searches and Yours Doesn't

8 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Programmatic SEO built Wise's 4.67 million monthly organic visits from one template. The difference between that and a Google spam penalty comes down to two things most indie builders skip: real data, and matching the page to what the searcher wants.

The trick behind Wise's traffic and NomadList's audience

Wise gets 4.67 million monthly organic searches from just 14,888 pages of currency-conversion content: one template, replicated across every currency pair that exists. Zapier runs around 800,632 integration pages, each showing how two specific apps connect, for roughly 306,000 monthly visits. NomadList's city guides, internet speed, cost of living, average temperature, cover tens of thousands of destinations the same way. None of these teams wrote each page by hand. They built one template and a database, and let the combinations do the work. This is programmatic SEO: generating keyword-targeted pages automatically instead of designing every page as a one-off, and it's the single most leveraged distribution move available to a small team building a niche site or app.

One ingredient decides whether it's SEO or spam

Google's John Mueller has warned plainly: "Programmatic SEO is often a fancy banner for spam." Google treats a templated page cluster as one pattern, not thousands of individual pages, so a weak template gets the whole site penalised at once rather than one page quietly ignored. The difference between Wise's traffic and a spam filter is a genuine, page-specific data point plus real usefulness to whoever lands there. Wise's exchange rate updates every day and is correct for that specific pair; that's real. A template that just swaps a city name into the same three paragraphs of filler text is exactly the pattern Google is now built to catch.

Intent decides the template, not the keyword list

Before you template anything, work out what the searcher wants, not just what they typed. The standard framework splits it four ways: informational (learning something), navigational (looking for a specific site), commercial (comparing options before buying), and transactional (ready to act right now). Someone searching "invoice template PDF free download" is showing transactional intent: they want the file or the tool immediately, not a thousand-word essay on invoicing best practice. Building a long blog post for a query like that, instead of a direct download or generator, is the most common reason an ambitious page cluster gets traffic that never converts to anything.

Where this meets your pricing model

Intent should also decide how you monetise the traffic once it arrives. A visitor who found a narrow, transactional calculator page is mid-task and low-commitment: a free tool that captures an email or nudges toward a light subscription once they want to save or export results fits that moment, better than a hard paywall before they've seen any value. A visitor who arrived via a commercial-intent roundup, already comparing several paid tools before clicking through, has already been primed to expect an ongoing, recurring-value product. They convert to a subscription more readily than the calculator visitor does, because the shopping behaviour that got them to you already assumes recurring value is normal. Matching pricing to the intent that brought the traffic matters as much as matching the page template to it.

The test before you template anything

Say you're building a rostering and quoting tool for small AU trades businesses. Before you build the engine that could generate five hundred location pages, "rostering software for Perth electricians", "rostering software for Brisbane plumbers", hand-write three of them yourself with real, category-specific detail rather than a swapped city name. Check two things over the following few weeks: does each page actually rank, and does a visitor who lands there do the thing you want rather than bounce. Only once that manual version works do you build the template that scales it. Skip straight to the database and you'll get thousands of pages and a manual action from Google, not thousands of pages and traffic.
