Westpac's 5% Investor Deposit Is a Hedge, Not a Gift

20 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

Westpac dropped its investor deposit to 5% the same week the Home Guarantee Scheme updated its own rules — one move tightens a subsidy, the other papers over a 34% forecast drop in investor lending, and the LMI bill tells you which is which.

Until this month, a 5% deposit belonged almost entirely to first home buyers holding a place in the government's Home Guarantee Scheme. On 17 July, Westpac opened the same number to investors: minimum deposit down from 10% to 5%, with lenders mortgage insurance covering the gap up to 95% LVR on principal-and-interest loans, and the maximum interest-only term stretched from 10 years to 15. Two "5% deposit" headlines landed in the same week, and they are not the same product. Knowing the difference is the first thing to check before recommending either.

What changed in the scheme

The scheme's own refresh took effect 1 July. Price caps moved in several markets: Darwin up from $600,000 to $750,000, regional New South Wales centres including the Illawarra, Newcastle and Lake Macquarie now capped at $1.5 million, Geelong at $950,000. Two other changes matter more for how a file actually gets processed. The retained-savings test tightened, applicants reserving a place from 1 July can't hold savings above six months of living costs plus six months of loan repayments after drawdown, with a 5% buffer on construction loans. And refinancing got easier: the inquiry-response window stretched from 30 to 42 days, and eligible refinance fees can now be capitalised into the new loan instead of paid upfront. ANZ is holding its own $30,000 retained-savings cap for now while it updates its systems, so the same client can clear the test at one lender and fail it at another until the majors catch up. The scheme itself, a government guarantee covering 15 percentage points of the loan above the standard 80% threshold (18 for single parents), has put more than 300,000 Australians into a home since launch.

Why Westpac moved the other lever

Westpac's change is a different animal. It isn't rationing a subsidised place; it's a risk-appetite call, and the timing explains why. From 1 July 2027, negative gearing will apply only to new-build purchases, and Westpac's own economists are forecasting investor activity to fall 34% in the near term. Loan Market was already reporting investor loan applications down 19% in June against pre-budget levels. A bank looking at a shrinking investor pipeline it can't fix has one lever left: lower the deposit hurdle for the buyers still willing to come in. The 5% headline reads the same as the government's, but it's solving the opposite problem: propping up volume rather than expanding access.

What the 5% actually costs

The difference shows up in lenders mortgage insurance, the mechanism doing the real work here. LMI covers the lender's loss if a high-LVR loan defaults and the sale doesn't clear the debt; the borrower pays the premium, either upfront or, under Westpac's new policy, capitalised into the loan balance. It's why a government-guaranteed 5% deposit and a bank's LMI-backed 5% deposit cost completely different amounts: the guarantee sits behind the gap for free, the premium doesn't. Canstar's published cost tables put a $600,000 purchase at 95% LVR at $31,008 in LMI, against $9,828 at 90% LVR and $5,100 at 85%, and that's the owner-occupier rate; lenders price investor LMI higher again. Scale the same table to a $700,000 purchase and the premium lands near $36,000 at 95% LVR — real money added to day-one debt, not a rounding error. Capitalising it, rather than requiring it in cash, is the only thing that makes a genuinely 5%-down investor purchase serviceable.

Route the client, not the headline

For a first home buyer inside the scheme's caps, 5% down means no LMI at all — the guarantee absorbs that risk, which is the entire point of a scheme now tight enough to police retained savings to the dollar. For an investor going through Westpac at 95% LVR, 5% down means paying for LMI, and now financing it too. Run the LMI number alongside the LVR number before recommending either path. Neither CBA nor NAB had matched Westpac's investor policy as of this week; whether they follow before the negative-gearing reform bites on 1 July 2027, or leave Westpac carrying the investor volume alone, is the thing to check every time a new lender rate sheet lands.
