PYS and PMIF Saved Under 1% of a Balance and Broke the Pool

22 July 2026 · synthesised from 1 source

Run the numbers behind Protecting Your Super and Putting Members' Interests First and the balance protection comes to under 1% of a typical payout, while the opt-in switch adversely selected the shrunken pool of members who kept cover and made everyone who lost it invisible to every experience study since.

Run the compounding arithmetic that justified Protecting Your Super (PYS) and Putting Members' Interests First (PMIF) and it holds up: a young member's default premium really does cost roughly nine times its face value in forgone retirement balance by 67. Run the insurance arithmetic on the same reform and the picture flips. The balance it protected is worth under 1% of a typical payout, and the opt-in switch that delivered it shrank and adversely selected the one pool group life exists to serve.

The case that was right

The question every actuary who backed these reforms would ask is a fair one: why should a 19-year-old casual worker, who will probably never claim, spend forty-five years compounding a policy sitting on autopilot? Before 2019, default cover ran from the day a super account opened, regardless of age or balance, and funds had let sums insured and duplicate accounts drift for years without checking whether the member wanted, or could even use, what they were paying for. PYS forced a 16-month-inactivity cancellation unless the member opted in; PMIF, from April 2020, extended that to any new account for a member under 25 or holding a balance under $6,000. The logic was sound, and the problem was real: years of default premiums quietly eating small, inactive balances.

Take the compounding case at face value. A typical default death-and-TPD premium for a member in their twenties runs somewhere around $150 a year, indicative and in line with scaled default cover for that age band. Invested instead at a 5% real return from 22 to 67, forty-five years, that single year's premium compounds to about nine times its size: 1.05⁴⁵ ≈ 8.99. Carry that through the first three years of a low-balance account, before employer contributions typically push it past the $6,000 threshold, and the forgone balance at retirement comes to roughly $3,850. Restate that one number three ways against a typical $450,000 balance at retirement: an absolute $3,850, about $1.65 a week across the member's working life, and 0.86% of their final balance: under 1%, however you cut it.

Insurance is a pool, not a savings top-up

But the ledger the reforms argued from, cost to the member who never claims, is only half the entry. Death and TPD don't wait for a member to turn 25 or their balance to clear $6,000, and for the ones an event does hit, twenty-something default cover is often the only cover they'll ever hold, because retail underwriting at that age in a manual or shift-work occupation is expensive or simply declined. Price that side with the same rigour. An indicative combined death-and-TPD incidence for members in their twenties, broadly consistent with typical group scheme experience for that age band, runs around 0.08% a year. Against a $200,000 default sum insured, that's an expected claim cost of about $160 a member a year: close enough to the $150 premium to confirm the pool was priced correctly, not sold as a bad deal. That small headroom, premium a touch above pure expected cost, is the ordinary loading for expenses and profit margin, not evidence the young cohort was ever overcharged. The 99.92% who pay and never claim in a given year aren't being taken for a ride. They're funding the 0.08% who are the reason the product exists.

The mechanism the reform's modelling never priced

Opt-in and opt-out are not neutral framings of the same choice. The default effect is one of the best-replicated findings in behavioural economics: flip a benefit from automatic to opt-in and participation among the previously-covered population collapses, typically to a low double-digit or single-digit share, almost regardless of how good the benefit is. Apply that to a pool and the mechanism changes character entirely. If default participation among under-25s and low-balance members settles at an indicative 10%, the members who opt back in are not a random 10% of the original group. They skew toward the ones who already suspect they need cover, whether that's a family history, a known condition, a riskier occupation, or simply the more engaged minority who reads the notice at all. That's opt-in adverse selection, and it runs the wrong way for the members left in the pool: a smaller base carrying a higher average claims rate. Nudge the incidence up by an indicative 25% to reflect that skew — 0.0010 rather than 0.0008 — and the expected claim cost per remaining member rises to about $200, a third above the original premium, before the fixed cost of administering the scheme gets reloaded across a membership a tenth of its former size. The members who most needed a cheap, pooled default now face a pricier, thinner one.

The population no experience study will ever see

Here's the part that doesn't show up in anyone's numbers. A pricing team building next year's mortality and TPD assumptions from a fund's claims experience is, by construction, working only from members who still hold a policy. A 23-year-old who lost default cover under PMIF and suffered a TPD event eighteen months later generates no claim, no decline, no record of any kind; they simply aren't in the dataset. That isn't a gap at the edge of an experience study; it's a population the study was never built to observe. For a book this size, that's not tail noise. It's the exact cohort the reform was meant to protect, absent from the one dataset that could say whether the trade-off paid off.

What to change inside the fund, not the Act

None of this argues for unwinding PYS or PMIF — the balance-erosion problem they targeted was real, if considerably smaller than the headlines suggested. It argues for a narrower fix inside a trustee's own insurance strategy. Scaled default cover keeps a smaller, cheaper sum insured opt-out for the affected cohort; it recovers most of the balance protection without triggering the participation collapse that a binary opt-in produces, and a handful of funds already run versions of it. For an appointed actuary signing off on next year's insurance strategy, or building the experience study behind it, the more immediate move is to model the shadow population explicitly: apply population-level mortality and TPD tables to the age and balance bands the fund pushed out of default cover, rather than treating them as out of scope. That estimate, and a scaled-cover costing to sit alongside it, fits inside the same review cycle as next year's insurance strategy paper: it needs a spreadsheet, not a Treasury consultation. You can't reserve for a risk you can't see. You can at least stop pricing as if it isn't there.
