The Life Insurance Code Gets 85 New Teeth — Kell's Final Report Lands

30 June 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

Peter Kell's final review of the Life Insurance Code of Practice lands with 85 recommendations — up from 72 in April — centred on mental health claims handling, plus same-day evidence that code governance committees are actively enforcing, not just writing rules.

Peter Kell handed down his final review of the Life Insurance Code of Practice on 30 June 2026 — and it's bigger than the interim report flagged. The April draft proposed 72 changes; the final report lands on 85 recommendations, with the Council of Australian Life Insurers (CALI) now on the hook to implement them and industry required to respond by 30 September 2026.

The headline issue: mental health

Kell is explicit that "the most challenging issues arose in relation to the life insurance industry's approach to dealing with mental health conditions and the increase in claims in this area." Two recommendations matter most for anyone pricing or reserving group life business:

  • A ban on total exclusions of mental health conditions in standard contracts — insurers can no longer simply carve mental health out altogether.
  • Any remaining limitation on mental health cover must be "supported by documented actuarial or statistical data" — not underwriter judgement or industry convention, but a defensible evidence base.

What it means for you: if a pricing or product team currently relies on broad mental health exclusions, or loadings justified by convention rather than data, that basis now needs to survive a code-compliance audit. Expect more demand for credible mental health claims-experience data — and for actuaries who can build and document it defensibly.

Codes vs prudential regulation — a distinction worth being precise about

It's easy to conflate this with APRA's prudential oversight, but they're different regulatory layers. APRA's quarterly statistics and capital standards govern solvency — can the insurer pay claims. A Code of Practice is industry self-regulation: a set of conduct standards the industry writes and commits to, policed not by APRA but by a Code Governance Committee (CGC) — an independent body that audits compliance, issues breach determinations, and can refer serious breaches to ASIC. Kell's review resets what the life code says; the CGC is what makes the new rules bite.

The CGCs are already enforcing, not just writing rules

Two general insurance code rulings landed the same day as Kell's report, and they show the enforcement appetite is real. The General Insurance Code Governance Committee found insurers failing to clearly disclose pricing and payment-option details in renewal notices — a premium transparency gap. Separately, a compliance review of strata insurance broking issued nine breach determinations and referred two brokers to ASIC. Neither involves life insurance directly, but both confirm the same pattern across the conduct-regulation landscape: code committees are moving from passive standard-setting to active, evidence-based enforcement, with ASIC referral as a real consequence — not a hypothetical one.

What to watch

CALI's implementation consultation through Q3 will determine how "documented actuarial or statistical data" gets defined in practice — that's the detail that will actually shape pricing and reserving work, and it's the kind of standard-setting global practice (UK and Canadian conduct codes have moved in a similar direction on mental health underwriting) is worth tracking against. If you sit anywhere near group life mental health claims, 30 September is the date to have a position ready by.
