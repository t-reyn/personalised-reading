Aperture, ISO, and the Shutter Speed Rule Unique to Video

3 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

The three controls that expose every shot — and the one rule, specific to video, that photography tutorials rarely mention.

Every shot you'll ever frame is exposed by the same three controls — aperture, shutter speed, and ISO — but one of them behaves differently the moment you switch from stills to video, and most beginner guides skip it entirely.

The three dials

Aperture is the opening in the lens that light passes through, measured in f-stops (f/1.4, f/2.8, f/16). A wider aperture lets in more light and produces a shallow depth of field — the blurred background you see behind an in-focus subject. A narrower aperture lets in less light but keeps more of the frame sharp front to back.

Shutter speed is how long the sensor is exposed for each frame, measured in fractions of a second. Slower shutter speeds let in more light but blur anything moving; faster speeds freeze motion but darken the image.

ISO is how sensitive the sensor is to the light it receives. Push it up and a dim shot brightens — but you trade that brightness for visible grain, or noise. These three controls are interchangeable in the sense that you can lose a stop of light on one and claw it back on another, but each carries its own visual side effect, and that trade-off is the whole game.

The rule that only applies once the camera is rolling

In photography, shutter speed is mostly a creative choice. In video, it's constrained by a formula: the 180-degree rule. Set your shutter speed to roughly double your frame rate. Shoot at 24 frames per second and your shutter speed should sit near 1/48s; at 30fps, near 1/60s; at 60fps, near 1/120s.

The reason is motion blur. That doubled ratio produces the amount of blur-per-frame our eyes expect from real-world motion — smooth, not stuttery. Push the shutter speed much faster than the rule (say 1/500s at 24fps) and movement starts to look unnaturally sharp and robotic, sometimes called strobing: a hand waving looks like a series of freeze-frames rather than a continuous motion. Go much slower than the rule and each frame smears, giving footage a blurry, dragging quality. The 180-degree rule is the midpoint that keeps frames sharp individually while motion still reads as fluid between them.

This is the twist that separates video exposure from photo exposure: shutter speed is no longer a free variable you adjust purely for light or effect. Once you've picked a frame rate, the shutter speed that looks natural is largely fixed.

Where that pushes the other two dials

Because shutter speed is locked by the 180-degree rule, a video shooter leans harder on aperture and ISO to manage exposure — the reverse of a lot of stills advice, which often treats shutter speed as the easiest dial to move. Say you're filming handheld indoors at 24fps in dim light: shutter speed is already set near 1/48s by the rule, so first open the aperture as wide as your lens and the depth of field you want will allow. If the shot is still too dark once the aperture is maxed out, ISO is what's left — and this is where the ISO-noise trade-off bites. Raising ISO brightens the image, but past a certain point (which varies by camera and sensor size) the extra grain becomes visible in shadows and flat areas first.

The practical order, most times you're stuck for light: fix shutter speed to the 180-degree rule, open the aperture to taste, then raise ISO only as far as the shot needs — checking your shadows for grain before you go further.

Try it on the next thing you shoot

Before you press record, pick your frame rate first — it decides your shutter speed, not the other way around. Then let aperture set your look and ISO do whatever brightness work is left. That order alone will fix the two most common beginner problems in handheld video: robotic-looking motion from too fast a shutter, and noisy low-light shots from reaching for ISO before the aperture's been opened up.
