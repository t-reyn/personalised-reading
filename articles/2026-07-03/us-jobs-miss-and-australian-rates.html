The US Jobs Miss That's Quietly Repricing Australian Mortgage Rates

3 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

US payrolls badly missed forecasts overnight and Fed rate-hike odds collapsed — a shift that reaches Australian bank funding costs before the RBA does anything at all.

US non-farm payrolls rose just 57,000 in June, barely half the 110,000 economists expected, while the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% only because fewer people were looking for work. Annual wage growth held at 3.5%. Within hours, traders had slashed the odds of a July Fed rate hike from a near coin-toss to about 22%, with a hold now priced at 78%. Bond yields and the US dollar fell as rate-hike bets unwound, and stock futures jumped — small-caps hardest, since they're the most rate-sensitive corner of the market.

Why a US number moves your mortgage before the RBA does

Australian banks don't fund home loans purely from local deposits — a meaningful slice comes from wholesale markets, where they borrow in bulk, often offshore, and swap the proceeds back into Australian dollars. This is monetary policy transmission's less-discussed cousin: it isn't only the RBA cash rate moving through the system, it's the global price of money moving through Australian bank balance sheets. When US bond yields fall because markets expect the Fed to hold or cut, offshore funding gets cheaper for Australian banks too — even though the RBA hasn't touched a thing. That's why swap rates and bank bill rates can drift ahead of an actual RBA decision: they're pricing in where global funding is heading, not just the next domestic board meeting.

Put concretely: a bank funding a fifth of its mortgage book offshore, seeing that funding cost ease 20 basis points on a global repricing, can shave a sliver off new loan pricing or fatten its margin — without the RBA lifting a finger.

The RBA can't just copy the Fed's homework

Even so, the Reserve Bank won't simply follow Washington's lead, because its mandate is Australia-specific inflation targeting: keep trimmed-mean inflation — the measure that strips out the most volatile price swings at either end of the distribution — within 2–3% over the medium term, using domestic data. The US jobs report is a story about a goods-heavy labour market cooling; Australia's stickiest inflation has been running through services — rent, insurance, out-of-pocket health costs — categories a US payrolls miss does nothing to touch. If the next domestic trimmed-mean print stays firm while US data keeps softening, the two central banks can diverge for months. Australian borrowers shouldn't assume a Fed cut fast-tracks a local one.

The rally underneath all this still needs earnings to show up

The jobs miss landed on top of an equity market already leaning on a large assumption: that AI-driven earnings growth justifies today's valuations. Nucleus Wealth's Damien Klassen argues the sharper question isn't "is there a bubble" but "is there an earnings bubble" — forward earnings are tracking roughly 25% growth for 2026 and 18% for 2027, with memory-chip sector revenue alone projected to triple from around US$200 billion in 2025 to near US$600 billion in 2026 and US$800 billion by 2027. His three risks to watch: inflation re-accelerating, an oil price shock, or an earnings downturn that compresses both profit forecasts and the multiple investors are willing to pay, at the same time.

What this morning's numbers actually show

As of 6:55am AEST, the ASX 200 sat at 8,724.5, down 0.6% for the session, while the VIX — Wall Street's fear gauge — eased 2.7% to 16.15, hardly the reading you'd expect if a bubble were bursting. Gold pushed up 1.6% to US$4,133.70/oz and the Australian dollar firmed to US$0.6925. That's a market repricing rate odds, not one fleeing risk.

Where to look next

If you're refinancing or locking in a rate, watch bank funding costs and swap rates over the coming weeks rather than waiting on an RBA announcement — offshore repricing can move faster than the Board does. And when the next trimmed-mean CPI print lands, that's the number that tells you whether Australia is decoupling from the US cycle or following it with a lag.
