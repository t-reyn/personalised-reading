The IFRS 17 CSM, in plain terms

25 June 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

The contractual service margin is how IFRS 17 stops insurers booking profit on day one — and spreads it over the life of the cover instead.

Under IFRS 17 a profitable contract is not allowed to show its profit at inception. Instead, the expected future profit is parked in a balance-sheet liability called the contractual service margin (CSM), and released to the income statement gradually as the insurer provides cover.

At initial recognition you estimate the present value of future cash flows, add a risk adjustment, and whatever profit remains becomes the opening CSM. Each period, a slice of it is released in line with the coverage units provided — a proxy for how much service the contract delivered that period. For a group life scheme, that release tracks the cover in force over the year rather than landing all at once when the contract is signed.

The subtlety that trips people up: the CSM is a shock-absorber. Changes in future-service assumptions — say, a revised mortality basis — adjust the CSM rather than hitting profit immediately, so earnings stay smooth. But changes relating to past or current service, and any onerous-contract losses, go straight through the P&L. Knowing which bucket a movement falls into is most of understanding an IFRS 17 result.
