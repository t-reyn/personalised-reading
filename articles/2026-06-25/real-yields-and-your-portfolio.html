What rising real yields mean for your portfolio

25 June 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Real yields are the interest rate after inflation — and when they rise, almost every asset you own has to re-price.

You already know that a bond's price falls when its yield rises. The piece that drives markets, though, is the real yield — the yield left over once you strip out expected inflation. A 5% nominal yield with 3% inflation is only a 2% real yield; the same 5% with 1% inflation is a 4% real yield, and that gap matters enormously.

Real yields are effectively the "risk-free" return on your money in real terms, so they act as gravity for everything else. When real yields climb, a safe inflation-protected bond starts to look attractive on its own, and riskier assets — long-duration tech stocks, gold, speculative growth — have to offer more to compete. That's why a jump in real yields can pull down assets that seem to have nothing to do with bonds.

For a long-term portfolio the takeaway isn't to trade every wiggle, but to understand why a broad sell-off is happening. If real yields are the cause, the move is a re-pricing of the discount rate, not a verdict on any single company. Knowing that helps you sit still when sitting still is the right call.
