Why retrieval beats fine-tuning for most apps

25 June 2026 · foundations piece

When you need an LLM to know your data, reach for retrieval before fine-tuning — it is cheaper, fresher, and easier to debug.

The instinct, when a model doesn't know about your product or your documents, is to fine-tune it on them. For most applications that's the wrong first move. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) keeps the model fixed and instead, at query time, looks up the most relevant chunks of your data and pastes them into the prompt as context. The model then answers from material it can see in front of it.

Because you already work with embeddings to find those chunks, the retrieval step is the easy part: store your documents as vectors, find the nearest few to the question, and hand them over. Nothing about the model changes, so there is no training run to pay for and nothing to re-do when a document is updated — you just re-index.

That is the real argument for retrieval over fine-tuning: freshness and debuggability. When an answer is wrong, you can read exactly which passages were retrieved and see whether the problem was bad retrieval or bad reasoning. Fine-tuning bakes knowledge into opaque weights, where the same mistake is far harder to trace. Reach for fine-tuning when you need to change the model's behaviour or format, not when you simply need it to know more facts.
