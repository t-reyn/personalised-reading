The Two Numbers That Tell You If Your App Is Actually Working

11 July 2026 · foundations piece

A side project can gain users every week and still lose money on each one. Customer acquisition cost and the LTV:CAC ratio are the two numbers that tell the difference — and why the manual, unscalable acquisition phase is what makes them trustworthy.

A side project can add users every week and still be losing money on each one — the tell isn't in the growth chart, it's in two numbers most indie builders never sit down and calculate: customer acquisition cost (CAC), and the ratio it needs to clear against lifetime value (LTV).

What a user actually costs you

CAC is your total spend on acquiring customers over a period, divided by the number of paying customers that spend produced. Spend $900 on a month of ads and convert 60 paying subscribers, and your CAC is $15. On its own, that number means nothing — it only becomes useful once you weigh it against what each customer is worth back, which is exactly where most builders stop measuring, because the growth chart already looks good.

The ratio, not the CAC figure alone, is what matters

LTV is the total revenue you expect from an average customer over the full relationship — for a subscription, that's roughly monthly price × expected months before they churn. A $10-a-month habit-tracking app losing 5% of subscribers monthly keeps the average one for about 20 months, so LTV lands near $200. Against a $15 CAC, that's a 13:1 ratio — comfortably clear of the rough rule of thumb that a healthy consumer subscription wants LTV:CAC at 3:1 or better. Below that line, you're funding growth you can't actually afford, even while the user count keeps climbing.

Run the same maths on a one-time-purchase product — a $39 tool with no renewal to compound — and LTV is just the $39 itself, so a $15 CAC only clears a 2.6:1 ratio: technically profitable, but with far less room to absorb a bad month of ad spend or a slow conversion funnel.

Manual acquisition changes the equation entirely

This is where messaging real users individually earns its keep beyond just validating the idea — it's close to $0 CAC in cash terms, even though it costs real hours. A founder who spends a week manually finding thirty users in the right Discord or Facebook group, rather than buying thirty clicks, doesn't need to clear any LTV:CAC threshold at all on that batch — the constraint becomes their own time, not an ad budget. That's exactly why the slow, unscalable phase still matters once paid channels are affordable: it buys real conversion and churn numbers to calculate an honest LTV before real money goes into finding out whether a paid CAC clears the bar.

Do this before your next ad dollar

Before spending anything on acquisition this month, calculate your actual monthly churn from whichever users you already have, convert it into an expected LTV, and work out the maximum CAC you can afford at a 3:1 ratio. If there aren't yet enough paying users to measure churn honestly, that's the signal to keep acquiring the slow way a little longer — a wrong LTV estimate is worse than no estimate, because it tells you to spend against a number that doesn't exist yet.
