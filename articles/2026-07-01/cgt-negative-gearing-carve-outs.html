Negative Gearing Reform Is Law — What the Carve-Outs Actually Change

1 July 2026 · synthesised from 7 sources

The negative gearing and CGT reform has passed — then the government announced small-business carve-outs and denied it's a backdown. What's actually different, what isn't, and what to check before you act.

The negative gearing and capital gains tax reform that's been reshaping how Australians think about property investing has now formally cleared Parliament and become law. Almost immediately, budget backlash forced the government to announce carve-outs aimed at small business — while the Treasurer insisted, pointedly, that this isn't a backdown. Two things can be true at once: the law is real, and the fine print just moved. Here's what actually changed this week, what didn't, and what's worth checking before you act on any of it.

The reform, briefly

Two mechanics are doing the work. First, negative gearing is now quarantined for established residential property bought after the commencement date — the rental loss can no longer offset your other income, only future capital gains on that same asset. New-build purchases keep the full benefit. Second, the CGT discount on investment property drops from 50% to 25% for anything acquired after commencement; holdings from before that date are grandfathered at the old 50%. That combination pushes the economics of the next purchase decisively toward new stock.

The backlash, and the carve-out

What's new since the law passed is the aftermath. Small business pushed back hard on how the reduced CGT discount interacts with owners selling active business assets — think a trades business, a clinic, a commercial premises an owner-operator sells to fund retirement — as distinct from a rental property. The government responded with additional exemptions aimed at that group. Albanese framed them as "generous"; the Treasurer separately denied the carve-outs amount to a backdown on the reform itself. The Guardian's read is blunter: it's "more tweak than transformation" — the core residential investor mechanics (new-build advantage, reduced discount on established stock, grandfathering) are unchanged. Read past the headline fight: nobody is disputing that the reform is real, only whether this particular adjustment counts as retreating from it.

A different regime, easy to conflate

This is the detail worth being precise about. Australia already runs a separate small business CGT concessions regime (Division 152 of the tax act) for owners disposing of active business assets — gated by tests on aggregated turnover or net asset value, with relief like the 15-year exemption, the 50% active asset reduction, a retirement exemption, and rollover relief. That's a genuinely different scheme from the investment-property CGT discount this reform just changed. The carve-out reported this week sits inside that small-business framework, not inside your rental property's tax treatment. Conflating "my investment property discount" with "my business asset concessions" is a common, and potentially costly, mix-up — they have different eligibility tests and different mechanics, and a change to one doesn't automatically touch the other.

What the market is already doing

Two signals worth watching, not acting on yet. realestate.com.au is reporting an investor exodus stripping hundreds of rental homes out of the Victorian market — landlords exiting ahead of, or because of, the changed math on established stock. Separately, the AFR reports money moving on the ASX as investors reposition ahead of the CGT change actually biting, effectively banking gains at the old 50% discount before their own commencement date arrives. Both are rational responses to a deadline — not signals to copy blind.

What to actually check

  • Your existing property's grandfathering. Current reporting keeps pre-commencement holdings at the 50% discount — but "commencement date" is the exact word that matters, not the headline summarising it. Confirm the date against the primary legislation or your accountant's summary before assuming anything about your own holding.
  • Don't borrow relief from the wrong scheme. If you're not disposing of an active business asset, the small-business carve-out isn't your carve-out — check which Division actually applies to what you hold.
  • New-build vs. established, for real this time. If a next purchase is on the table, the reform makes the new-build case (full negative gearing plus structural depreciation) materially stronger than it was a month ago — model both before you shop.
  • Wait out the first fortnight of coverage. A law that "passes" one week and gets "carved out" the next is a sign the implementing detail is still settling. Treat the first round of headlines as provisional and wait for ATO guidance or the explanatory memorandum before restructuring anything around it.

The bottom line

The reform is law, the core mechanics for residential investors are unchanged, and this week's carve-out is a small-business measure that most property investors will never touch. The market is already pricing the deadline in — you don't need to react to the headline cycle, just confirm the two dates that actually govern your own position: when you acquired, and when the rules commence.
