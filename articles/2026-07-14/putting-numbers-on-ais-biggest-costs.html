Putting Numbers on AI's Biggest Costs

14 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Two studies landed this week trying to quantify what AI actually costs — one on jobs, signed by 200+ economists and 16 Nobel laureates, one on emissions, from a Rice University team measuring how much of a data centre's carbon output could be buried underground.

AI's two biggest externalities — what it does to jobs and what it does to the grid — have mostly been argued in adjectives. This week both got numbers attached, from two unrelated research efforts that happened to land days apart.

A letter with 16 Nobel laureates attached

On 13 July, an open letter organised through Stanford University's digital economy lab collected more than 200 signatures from economists and AI researchers, including 16 Nobel laureates and executives from Anthropic, Google and OpenAI. The letter argues AI "could drive an unprecedented transformation of our economy, larger than the Industrial Revolution, but unfolding over a vastly shorter time frame," and names large-scale job displacement as the central risk alongside the upside of major gains in living standards. Yoshua Bengio, the Université de Montréal computer scientist who signed it, called the transformation "highly plausible" and specifically warned against leaving the adjustment to market forces alone — the letter's ask is that governments and industry build the incentives, guardrails and institutions before the disruption, not after.

What the letter doesn't do is put a number on how many jobs or how fast — deliberately. It's a statement of risk magnitude and urgency from people whose day job is normally to avoid exactly that kind of prediction, which is itself informative: economists reaching for "unprecedented" and "Industrial Revolution" as their comparison is a stronger signal than any single displacement estimate would be.

The other externality: what a data centre breathes out

Rice University's Hon Chung Lau and energy-transition consultant Steve Tsai took the opposite approach on AI's second externality — precise numbers, published this month in Energy & Fuels. Using public data on announced US data centres (power capacity, energy source, location), they modelled the sector's carbon output against the country's underground storage capacity. The baseline is stark: data-centre emissions run at roughly 90 million metric tonnes of CO2 a year in 2025, on track to exceed 404 million tonnes annually by 2030 as data-centre power capacity grows from 40 to 169 gigawatts.

Their finding is the counterweight: more than 90% of that CO2 could be captured and piped into underground saline aquifers, and 34 US states hold enough aquifer capacity between them to store over a century of projected data-centre emissions beyond 2030. By 2030 alone, aquifers in range of the announced data-centre buildout could plausibly hold around 299 million tonnes — 74% of that year's projected output.

Storage was never really the bottleneck

The gap between "90% could be captured" and "will be captured" is the part the study can't answer, and it's the same gap that's dogged carbon capture and storage for two decades: the geology has rarely been the constraint. Capturing CO2 at the point source, compressing it, and building pipeline infrastructure to a suitable aquifer costs real money per tonne, and unless a carbon price or a regulator makes that cost unavoidable, a data-centre operator racing to bring capacity online has no commercial reason to pay it. The Rice study is a proof that the physical headroom exists, not a forecast that it will be used — those are different claims, and the coverage of studies like this one routinely blurs them.

Two numbers, one pattern

Put side by side, the letter and the study make the same point from opposite ends: AI's costs are becoming measurable faster than the institutions meant to manage them are adapting. Emissions accounting has decades of regulatory machinery (carbon markets, disclosure rules, the aquifer geology itself) that simply isn't being applied yet at the pace data-centre capacity is being built. Labour markets have even less machinery in place — no equivalent of a "carbon price" exists for job displacement, which is exactly what the Stanford letter is asking governments to start designing.

The signal to track

Two things worth tracking rather than the letter or the study themselves: whether any US state moves to require or subsidise data-centre CO2 capture as a condition of new grid connections (that's the mechanism that would actually close the "could vs will" gap), and whether the Stanford letter produces a concrete policy proposal rather than a second open letter next quarter. Statements of concern from credentialed signatories are cheap to produce and easy to ignore; the tell for whether this one matters is whether it's followed by legislation, a levy, or a capture mandate within the next year.
