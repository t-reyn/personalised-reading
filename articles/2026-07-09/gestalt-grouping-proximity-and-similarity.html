Why Some Screens Feel Instantly Organised And Others Feel Like A Wall Of Buttons

9 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Two Nielsen Norman Group breakdowns of Gestalt grouping explain why some interfaces read as organised at a glance: proximity and similarity are doing the work before anyone reads a word, whether or not you designed for them.

Before anyone reads a single label on your screen, their eye has already decided which buttons belong together and which item is different from the rest. That decision happens through two Gestalt principles, proximity and similarity, and both work whether or not you designed for them, which is exactly why it's worth doing on purpose.

Proximity: spacing is structure, not decoration

The proximity principle is blunt: items placed close together read as one group, and a gap reads as "these are unrelated." Nielsen Norman Group's research gives a concrete case: split a 12-field form into three groups of four with visible spacing between them and it feels manageable; run the same 12 fields with even spacing throughout and it reads as one long, undifferentiated task. Nothing about the fields changed. Only the gaps did.

The failure mode runs the other way too. Bury an "Add" button among a cluster of unrelated actions, Next, Save, Cancel, sitting at equal spacing, and users assume everything at that same distance serves a similar purpose; the Add button effectively disappears into a row it doesn't belong in. Responsive layouts hit the same problem: a two-column desktop view that groups a field with its helper text can stack vertically on mobile in a way that separates them, so a relationship a user could see instantly on desktop has to be re-learned on the smaller screen.

Similarity: same look, same job

Similarity groups by shared visual traits rather than distance: colour, shape or size, even when the items sit far apart on the screen. Colour is the strongest of the three. Once you establish that a certain blue means "clickable," every element in that colour inherits the expectation, which is exactly why decorating a non-interactive icon in your link colour quietly weakens trust in every other link on the page. Shape carries the same weight: rounded pill buttons read as one family, usually navigation, rectangular buttons read as another, usually filters or actions, and mixing them without a reason sends users hunting for a pattern that isn't there. Size signals importance and grouping at once: a grid of product cards at identical size tells the eye "these are all equivalent options," while doubling one card's size correctly flags "this one is different." The same cue misfires when it's applied by accident, which is the mechanism behind banner blindness: content styled and sized like an ad gets scanned past like one, even when it's the thing the user actually needs.

The two principles are doing the job whitespace already started

This builds directly on the whitespace-and-hierarchy foundations from earlier: whitespace was never just breathing room between elements, it's the actual mechanism proximity uses to tell the eye where one group ends and the next begins. Similarity adds a second, independent channel, colour, shape, size, that can group things across distance where whitespace alone can't reach. Used together, deliberately, they do the organising work before a single word gets read; used by accident, they organise things anyway, just not the way you meant.

Audit your own screen in five minutes

Open any app on your phone, screenshot one screen, and without reading a single label, circle the groups your eye forms on its own. Then check what actually grouped them, spacing, colour or size, and whether that matches what the designer intended. Anywhere your circled group doesn't match the intended one is a proximity or similarity mismatch worth fixing before you touch colour or copy.
