Every Shot You Take Comes Down To Three Dials Fighting Each Other

9 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

Aperture, shutter speed and ISO trade off against each other in every frame you shoot, and video adds a fourth rule photography doesn't need. Here's how the three dials work, and why pushing ISO to fix a dark shot makes it look worse, not better.

Every camera you own, phone included, runs the same trade-off the instant you hit record: how much light gets in, and what you give up to control it. That trade-off has a name, the exposure triangle, and once you can feel it in your hands, "why does this look grainy" and "why is the background all one blur" stop being separate mysteries and become the same three-dial problem.

Three dials, three different costs

Aperture is the size of the opening light passes through, measured in f-stops. A wide aperture like f/1.4 lets in a flood of light but narrows what's in focus, throwing the background soft. A narrow aperture like f/16 keeps far more of the frame sharp but demands more light from somewhere else to compensate. Shutter speed is how long the sensor stays open per frame: it has a direct, 1:1 relationship with exposure, so doubling the exposure time doubles the light you capture, along with more motion blur on anything moving. ISO is the sensor's sensitivity to that light: pushing it up brightens the image without touching the other two dials, but every stop you add layers more visible noise, that grainy, speckled texture, into the shot.

The three trade off against each other exactly: f/22 at 16 seconds, f/4.0 at half a second, and f/1.4 at 1/15 second all capture identical total light. What changes is the look, deep and blur-free at one end, shallow and frozen at the other. That's the whole game: pick the creative effect you want, then choose which dial pays for it.

Video adds a rule photography doesn't need

In stills, shutter speed is yours to set freely. In video it isn't, because shutter speed also decides how natural motion looks from one frame to the next. The convention here is the 180-degree shutter rule: set your shutter speed to roughly double your frame rate. At 24fps that's 1/48, which rounds in practice to 1/50 (the maths works out to a 173-degree shutter angle, close enough to the 180-degree standard that it reads as normal motion blur to the eye). At 30fps, that's 1/60; at a 60fps slow-motion frame rate, 1/120.

Break that ratio deliberately and you get a known effect, not a mistake. A much faster shutter (a tighter angle) strips out motion blur and produces the stuttering, hyper-real look war and action films use for impact — Saving Private Ryan's Omaha Beach sequence is the textbook example. A much slower shutter smears motion into a dreamy blur. Neither is wrong; both are choices, but only once you know the 180-degree setting is the neutral baseline you're deviating from.

Why your "brighter" shot looks worse, not better

This is where the three dials collide in practice. Say you're filming indoors at dusk and the shot's too dark. You could open the aperture, but you already want the background in focus. You could slow the shutter, but you'd break your 180-degree ratio and blur the motion. That leaves ISO, and ISO is the one dial with no free lunch: every stop up adds noise, so a shot pushed to ISO 6400 to compensate for bad light will look brighter but grittier than the same scene lit properly and shot at ISO 400. The noise floor is the real cost of ISO, and no amount of colour grading fixes it afterward; it only makes the grain more visible.

Run the three-shot test before you touch anything else

Pick one static scene, lock your shutter at double your frame rate, and shoot it three times changing only ISO: once low, once mid, once as high as your camera goes. Look at the noise in the shadows on each. That's the exposure triangle stopping being a formula and starting to be something you can see, and it's the base layer every composition and colour-grading decision you make later sits on top of.
