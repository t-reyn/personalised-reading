Two Lenders Just Changed Leaders — Most Brokers Won't Notice In Time

5 July 2026 · synthesised from 3 sources

ANZ picked up a new board director from NAB's business bank and Bank of Sydney made its acting CEO permanent — plus the time-blocking habit that decides whether you're the broker who actually benefits from it.

ANZ picked up a new board director this week who used to run half of NAB's business bank, and Bank of Sydney just made its acting CEO permanent: two leadership changes that matter more to your pipeline than another look at the rate sheet, if you do something with them before the rest of the market does.

Who's in the seat now

Rachel Slade joins ANZ Group's board, along with the ANZ Group Holdings and ANZ BH boards, as a non-executive director from 13 July. She left NAB in mid-2025 after eight years, most recently as Group Executive for Business and Private Banking, covering SME, specialised banking, private banking and advisory. Before that, 17 years at Westpac across transactional, retail and business banking. ANZ chair Paul O'Sullivan pointed to her "customer-led strategy and large-scale transformation" record as the reason for the appointment.

At Bank of Sydney, Kieran McKenna's appointment as permanent CEO takes effect from 1 July, having run the bank as acting CEO since 3 November 2025. He joined in 2024 as chief risk officer after a decade running risk at the ASX, plus earlier senior roles at Westpac, St.George and Commonwealth Bank. The board specifically credited him with delivering the bank's core banking migration, onto the Infosys Finacle platform, "on time and within agreed parameters." That migration is now finished, not pending.

Why this is broker-relevant, not just bank trivia

Lender appetite and servicing policy get set, or at least blessed, at exactly this level, and a broker who's built even a thin relationship with a new decision-maker's office picks up policy exceptions and early appetite signal that anyone reading only rate sheets misses entirely. Slade's SME and private banking background is the tell for where ANZ's business-lending appetite could tilt next; a lender that's just finished a core banking migration, like Bank of Sydney, is typically the one about to get more aggressive on turnaround times and new product, because the platform work that was eating its attention is now done.

The habit that decides whether you're in the room

Jason Back, founder of Broker Essentials, argues in a 3 July interview that brokers who feel constantly busy but aren't growing have a prioritisation problem, not a time problem: "being busy isn't the same as being productive." His fix is time-blocking: dividing the week into protected segments for the small number of "$10,000-an-hour" activities, lender BDM catch-ups, referral-partner check-ins, pipeline review, rather than letting the calendar fill reactively with "$10" tasks that anyone could do. Even one protected hour a week of unstructured "CEO time," reviewing what's genuinely moving the business rather than the next file in the queue, beats reacting all day and hoping strategy happens by accident.

Put the two together and the point sharpens: a leadership reshuffle at two of your lenders is exactly the kind of high-value trigger that belongs in a protected block, not a "get to it eventually" pile alongside routine file admin. Brokers who treat every incoming task as equally urgent are the ones who'll still be processing this week's settlements when a competitor has already had a first call with the new decision-maker's office.

What to block out this week

Put 30 minutes on the calendar to email or call your ANZ BDM about what Slade's SME background could mean for upcoming deals, and check directly with Bank of Sydney whether turnaround times are moving now that the platform migration is done. Do it as a protected block, not a gap between files: that's the difference this piece is about.
