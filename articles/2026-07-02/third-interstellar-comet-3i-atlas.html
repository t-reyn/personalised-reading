The Third Interstellar Comet Turns Out to Be 12 Billion Years Old

2 July 2026 · synthesised from 2 sources

New James Webb spectroscopy on 3I/Atlas, only the third confirmed interstellar visitor, shows chemistry that doesn't match anything born in our solar system, and puts its age at up to 12 billion years.

The third confirmed interstellar visitor to pass through our solar system has turned out to be stranger than its unbound trajectory already suggested. Three studies published this month on comet 3I/Atlas, including one in Nature built on spectra from the James Webb Space Telescope, show its chemistry doesn't match anything that formed here, and put its age at up to 12 billion years: roughly two and a half times older than the Sun.

Found early, for once

3I/Atlas was first reported on 1 July 2025 by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile, with pre-discovery images tracing back to mid-June 2025. Its hyperbolic, unbound trajectory confirmed straight away that it wasn't a solar system native: the "I" in its name marks it interstellar, and the "3" that it's only the third ever confirmed. It reached perihelion on 30 October 2025 at about 1.4 astronomical units from the Sun, just inside Mars's orbit, passing no closer than 1.8 AU (around 170 million miles) from Earth. Hubble puts an upper limit on the nucleus at 5.6 kilometres across, though it may be as small as 440 metres.

A chemical fingerprint that doesn't match

Its coma carries the usual comet inventory: water, carbon monoxide, methane, cyanides, sulfides, plus something less usual, free-floating iron and nickel atoms, and a mix skewed heavily toward carbon dioxide over ammonia. What really sets it apart, though, is the ratio between its isotopes. 3I/Atlas's deuterium-to-hydrogen (D/H) ratio sits around 1%, well above every solar system comet measured so far, and its carbon isotope ratio (¹²C to ¹³C) falls outside the solar system's range entirely.

Why isotopes are the tell

Isotopes are versions of the same element with different numbers of neutrons: chemically near-identical, but physically distinguishable. When ice condenses out of a cloud of gas and dust, the exact ratio of those isotopes gets locked in, set by the temperature and make-up of that specific environment. Two comets built from the same basic ingredients, water, carbon, ammonia, can still carry very different isotope signatures if they formed in different places, at different temperatures, around different stars. That's what makes an isotope ratio a fingerprint rather than just an ingredients list: it tells you about the neighbourhood an object grew up in, not just what it's made of. Researchers estimate 3I/Atlas condensed at temperatures below 30 Kelvin (–243°C) in a protoplanetary disc that formed roughly 12 billion years ago, well before the Sun existed.

A second, independent clock

Age estimates like this rarely rest on one method, and this one didn't. Researchers cross-checked 3I/Atlas's age using its velocity relative to the galactic disc: objects that formed earlier in the galaxy's history tend to move faster relative to the Sun's local neighbourhood, a signature of having had longer to get gravitationally kicked around by passing stars over cosmic time. That analysis independently supports an age of at least 7 billion years, consistent with the isotope-derived estimate. Put together, 3I/Atlas isn't just visiting from another star — it's a physical sample of a protoplanetary disc that existed when the Milky Way looked very different, delivered to our doorstep for free.

A closing window

3I/Atlas is now outbound, ten months past perihelion and dimming as it recedes. Unlike a solar system comet on a repeating orbit, it will not swing back for a second look, and every telescope-hour left before it fades out of reach is data that can't be recollected later. Expect further JWST and ground-based spectroscopy over the coming months, aimed at refining the ice composition and testing the age estimate against independent methods. Only two interstellar objects were caught before this one, both on the way out with barely any lead time — 3I/Atlas's early detection is the first real test of how much a fast survey can learn from a visitor before it's gone for good.
